Project Overview

Churn & Retention Analytics is a full lifecycle system that takes synthetic subscription data from raw usage, billing, and support events to SQL features, ML risk scores, and a Streamlit retention simulator for ROI‑based intervention decisions.

Executive Summary

  • Business problem: subscription businesses need to know who is about to churn, which customers are worth saving, and which levers actually pay back.
  • What was built: a synthetic but realistic customer base (~100k), SQL feature pipeline, logistic regression + random forest models, and a Streamlit simulator that ties interventions to ROI.
  • Why it matters operationally: links churn scores to actual dollar outcomes instead of stopping at a ROC curve.
  • Key insights: 30‑day usage decline, payment issues, and support friction are strong churn drivers; plan tier and timing windows change the economics of intervention.
  • Indicative impact: the best simulated strategy focuses support‑style interventions on high‑value, high‑risk customers, achieving ~2.3× ROI vs blanket discounts.

Business Problem: Churn Risk & Retention ROI

Churn is a critical business problem for SaaS, telecom, fintech, and any recurring revenue model. Leaders ask:

  • Who is about to churn, and when?
  • Which customers are worth saving? (high value, high risk)
  • Which retention levers actually pay back? (discounts, support, features)

Many churn projects stop at a predictive model and never connect back to decisions. This project goes further by linking scores to segments, strategies, and ROI in a simulator that business users can understand.

Architecture, Data & Modeling

1. How the system is structured

The churn lab is designed like a real subscription analytics stack: a pipeline that starts with lifecycle‑shaped data, runs through SQL feature engineering, trains interpretable + stronger models, and ends in a decision tool that business users can touch. 

Raw Data → SQL Feature Engineering → EDA & Lifecycle Analysis → ML Models → Retention Simulator

End‑to‑end philosophy: The project connects raw data (usage logs, billing events, support tickets) to business decisions (which customers to target, which interventions to use, what ROI to expect). This is different from many churn projects that stop at a predictive model and never connect back to actions. The retention simulator bridges that gap by letting business users experiment with strategies and see dollar outcomes.

Repository layout:

  • pipeline/ – orchestration of data generation, SQL, and training. This is the entry point that runs the full pipeline from raw data to trained models, ensuring reproducibility.
  • sql/ – staging, feature tables, and data quality checks. SQL is the source of truth for features, making them auditable and reusable across models and analyses.
  • src/ – EDA helpers and supporting Python modules. Reusable functions for lifecycle analysis, cohort comparisons, and visualization.
  • models/ – stored model artifacts and metadata. Trained models are saved here so the simulator can load them without retraining. Metadata includes feature names, model version, and performance metrics.
  • app/ – Streamlit retention simulator. The interactive tool that business users interact with to explore strategies and ROI. This is what makes the project “live” rather than just a report.
  • docs/ – narrative per‑stage documentation and interview guide. Explains the business context, methodology, and how to present the project in interviews or stakeholder meetings.

2. Data Landscape & Lifecycle Modeling

Synthetic data is generated to match realistic subscription behavior. The event model is customer‑centric: each customer has usage logs (e.g., daily active usage), billing events (payments, dunning), and support tickets. Lifecycle stages (Onboarding, Active, At‑Risk, Churned) are derived from these logs and used as the backbone for analysis and strategy design.

  • ~92,610 customers.
  • Usage, billing, and support logs with seasonality and noise.
  • Plan tiers (Free, Basic, Pro, Enterprise) with different churn profiles.

Lifecycle stages are assigned based on usage trends, payment status, and support load so that the simulator and models can target “at‑risk” and “high‑value” segments consistently.

3. Metrics, KPIs & North Star

The churn lab is built around a small set of retention KPIs. The north star is retention ROI: net value (retained revenue minus intervention cost) per strategy. Secondary KPIs include baseline churn rate, model performance (e.g., ROC AUC, precision at top 10%), segment‑level churn rates, and intervention uptake. These are exposed in the Streamlit retention simulator so that business users can compare strategies and see ROI by segment.

4. SQL Feature Engineering & Data Quality

The sql/ layer turns raw usage, billing, and support tables into analysis‑ready features such as:

  • 30‑day usage trends: Rolling windows compute average daily usage over the past 30 days, with comparisons to prior periods (e.g., usage_30d vs usage_60d_ago) to flag declining engagement. These trends are normalized by plan tier (e.g., Enterprise users may have higher baseline usage) to avoid false positives.
  • Payment issue counts and dunning status: Aggregates failed payment attempts, dunning emails sent, and payment retry success rates. Customers with multiple payment failures in a short window are flagged as high‑risk, especially if they are on paid plans (Free tier users may not have payment data).
  • Support ticket volume and recency: Counts tickets opened in the past 30 days, time since last ticket, and ticket resolution time. High ticket volume or unresolved tickets correlate with churn risk, particularly if tickets are about billing or account issues rather than feature questions.
  • Lifecycle stage and tenure: Assigns customers to stages (Onboarding, Active, At‑Risk, Churned) based on usage patterns, payment status, and support load. Tenure (days since signup) is used as a control variable to separate lifecycle effects from time‑in‑service effects.
  • Plan tier and value: Encodes plan tier (Free, Basic, Pro, Enterprise) and computes monthly recurring revenue (MRR) per customer. High‑value customers (e.g., Enterprise) are prioritized in retention strategies, while low‑value customers (e.g., Free) may be allowed to churn naturally if intervention costs exceed retained value.

Data quality checks: The SQL layer includes validation queries that check for missing values, outliers, and logical inconsistencies (e.g., churned customers with future usage events). These checks are run before model training to ensure features are clean and reliable.

5. Modeling & Explainability

Two models are trained for risk scoring: an interpretable Logistic Regression baseline and a stronger Random Forest for performance. SHAP is used to communicate global feature importance to stakeholders (usage trends, payment issues, support load, plan tier).

Logistic Regression baseline: Trained with L2 regularization to prevent overfitting. Coefficients are interpretable: a positive coefficient for “usage_30d_decline” means declining usage increases churn risk. This model serves as a baseline and is useful for stakeholders who want to understand “why” a customer is flagged, not just “what” the score is.

Random Forest for performance: Trained with 100 trees, max depth 10, and min samples per leaf 5. This model achieves higher ROC AUC (0.96 vs 0.89 for Logistic Regression) and better precision at top 10% risk (51% vs 38%). The Random Forest captures non‑linear interactions (e.g., payment issues + support load together create higher risk than either alone) that Logistic Regression misses.

Model Training & SHAP Explainer (Python)

This Python script demonstrates the class-balanced Random Forest classifier setup and SHAP explainer computation: 

from sklearn.ensemble import RandomForestClassifier
from sklearn.model_selection import train_test_split
from sklearn.metrics import roc_auc_score
import shap

# Step 1: Split dataset into train and test sets
X = df.drop(columns=['customer_id', 'churn_label'])
y = df['churn_label']
X_train, X_test, y_train, y_test = train_test_split(X, y, test_size=0.20, random_state=42, stratify=y)

# Step 2: Configure Class-Balanced Random Forest
rf_model = RandomForestClassifier(
    n_estimators=150,
    max_depth=10,
    min_samples_leaf=5,
    class_weight="balanced",
    random_state=42,
    n_jobs=-1
)
rf_model.fit(X_train, y_train)

# Step 3: Evaluate model performance
y_pred_proba = rf_model.predict_proba(X_test)[:, 1]
auc_score = roc_auc_score(y_test, y_pred_proba)
print(f"Random Forest Test ROC AUC: {auc_score:.4f}")

# Step 4: Compute SHAP values for explaining predictions
explainer = shap.TreeExplainer(rf_model)
shap_values = explainer.shap_values(X_test)

# Plot summary representation
shap.summary_plot(shap_values[1], X_test, max_display=10)

SHAP explainability: SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) values are computed for the Random Forest to show global feature importance and per‑customer contributions. Global importance shows that usage decline, payment issues, and support load are the top drivers. Per‑customer SHAP values help customer success teams understand why a specific customer is flagged (e.g., “this customer has a 60% churn risk because usage declined 40% in the past 30 days and they have 2 unresolved support tickets”).

Model validation: Models are evaluated on a holdout set (20% of data) with ROC AUC, precision‑recall curves, and calibration plots. The Random Forest is well‑calibrated (predicted probabilities match observed churn rates), which is important for the retention simulator to estimate ROI accurately.

6. Retention Strategy Simulator (Streamlit)

The Streamlit retention simulator is where scores become decisions. Business users can:

  • Filter by segment (plan, region, lifecycle stage).
  • Set risk thresholds and choose interventions (discount, support, feature unlock).
  • Estimate churn reduction per strategy.
  • Compute intervention cost and retained revenue → net ROI.

This is surfaced in the portfolio via the streamlit_url field when deployed, turning the project into a live, explorative tool rather than a static report.

Analysis, Insights & Business Impact

7. Observations & Key Results

  • Baseline churn: ~7.58% across the synthetic portfolio. This is realistic for a mid‑sized SaaS product where churn rates typically range from 5–10% monthly. The rate varies by plan tier: Free tier churn is ~12%, while Enterprise churn is ~3%, reflecting the higher switching costs and value for enterprise customers.
  • Random Forest performance: ROC AUC 0.96, Precision@Top 10% ≈ 51%. The model achieves strong discrimination (ROC AUC near 1.0 means it can separate churners from non‑churners well) and high precision at the top risk decile (51% of top 10% risk customers actually churn, vs 7.58% baseline). This means customer success teams can focus on a small, high‑risk segment and expect roughly half to churn if not intervened.
  • Top 10% risk customers: ~51% churn probability vs 7.58% baseline. This is a 6.7× lift in churn rate, meaning the model successfully identifies customers who are much more likely to churn than average. The top 10% risk segment includes roughly 9,200 customers (10% of ~92k), so targeting this segment is operationally feasible.
  • Best ROI strategy: priority support on high‑value customers yields ~2.3× ROI in the simulator. This strategy targets customers in the top 20% by MRR (monthly recurring revenue) and top 10% by churn risk, then applies a “priority support” intervention (estimated cost $50 per customer, retention lift 15%). The simulator computes: retained revenue = MRR × retention_lift × 12 months, intervention cost = $50 × customers targeted, ROI = (retained revenue − intervention cost) / intervention cost. The 2.3× ROI means for every $1 spent on interventions, $2.30 is retained in revenue.
  • Feature importance insights: Usage decline (30‑day trend) is the strongest predictor, followed by payment issues and support load. Plan tier and tenure also matter, but less than behavioral signals. This suggests that monitoring usage trends and payment health is more actionable than trying to change plan tier or tenure.

8. Trade-offs, Risks & Failure Modes

The churn lab makes deliberate trade-offs: synthetic data may not reflect real subscription behavior; the simulator uses simplified cost and retention assumptions. Model performance on synthetic data may not generalize to production. Mitigation: validate features and model on a holdout or real cohort; tune intervention costs and retention lift with domain input before scaling strategies.

9. Executive Insights & Business Impact

  • Usage decline is the strongest signal. Declining 30‑day usage is a more powerful predictor than static attributes alone, supporting lifecycle‑based reactivation campaigns.
  • Plan tier structures risk. Free/Basic tiers are much riskier than Enterprise, which supports tier‑specific strategies and SLAs.
  • Payment and support friction compound churn. Payment issues and heavy support load together create a 4.5× higher‑risk segment in synthetic data.
  • Intervention timing matters. The 31–45 day window provides a meaningful chance to intervene before churn becomes irreversible.

10. Recommendations

  • Prioritize high‑value, high‑risk segments for human‑touch interventions; let low‑value, low‑risk churn naturally.
  • Operationalize usage‑trend monitoring as an early‑warning system for at‑risk customers.
  • Integrate with CRM/billing systems to run the simulator on real portfolios.
  • Automate pipeline scheduling via Airflow/DBT/Jobs in a production setting.

11. Limitations & What We'd Improve

Data is synthetic; production would connect to real usage, billing, and support systems. The simulator uses simplified cost and retention assumptions; we would calibrate with real intervention and retention data. The pipeline has no scheduling or orchestration; a production deployment would add Airflow, DBT, or similar for daily/weekly runs.

12. Future Scope (12–24 Months)

  • Connect the simulator to real CRM/billing data and calibrate retention lift and cost per intervention.
  • Add multi‑touch and time‑varying intervention strategies (e.g., first email, then support call if no response).
  • Introduce drift monitoring on feature distributions and model performance over time.
  • Operationalize the pipeline with scheduling (Airflow/DBT) and alerting for at‑risk segments.

Conclusion

Churn & Retention Analytics demonstrates that a full lifecycle system—from raw usage and billing data to SQL features, ML risk scores, and a retention simulator—can link churn scores to actual dollar outcomes instead of stopping at a ROC curve. By tying interventions to ROI and surfacing the best strategy (e.g., priority support on high‑value, high‑risk customers), the design gives business users a clear path from analytics to decisions. For reviewers, the fastest understanding path is: skim this article, then open the simulator to experiment with thresholds and strategies—seeing how analytics and ML translate to specific, measurable churn decisions.
