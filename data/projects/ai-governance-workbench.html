Project Overview

The AI Governance Workbench is a SQL‑ and Streamlit‑first control room for AI governance teams: it combines risk dashboards, document summarization, compliance audits, and a Gemini + RAG assistant into one place so policy owners can see AI system health and answer “are we covered?” in minutes instead of weeks.

Executive Summary

  • Business problem: AI/LLM deployments move fast, but governance still runs on PDFs, spreadsheets, and email threads, making it hard to see what is in scope, which controls are missing, and who owns the risk.
  • What was built: a multi‑module Streamlit workbench with governance KPIs, a data visualizer, policy summarizer + flashcards, a compliance audit prototype, and a Gemini‑backed Q&A assistant grounded in uploaded documents.
  • Why it matters operationally: turns static AI policies into a repeatable workflow for reviews, evidence collection, and go/no‑go decisions across many systems.
  • Scale & realism: simulated but structured tables for systems, policies, controls, evidence, and incidents that mirror what real governance teams track.
  • Key insights: governance can be productized, LLMs are most useful in the “last mile” (summaries, explanations), and KPIs like incident aging and evidence coverage are as important as traditional model metrics.
  • Indicative impact: a workbench like this can materially reduce time‑to‑review and improve evidence coverage, giving leaders a clearer, auditable view of AI risk without hiring a large new team.

Business Problem: Fragmented AI Governance Workflows

Most organizations now run pilots or production workloads with AI and LLMs, but governance has not kept up. Policies live in PDFs on shared drives, model cards sit in separate tools, and incident logs and evidence are scattered across email threads, tickets, and spreadsheets.

  • No single view of AI systems in scope for a given policy, framework, or regulation.
  • Manual, slow reviews where teams read long documents and try to remember what was checked last time.
  • Poor evidence coverage: it is hard to see which controls have concrete artifacts attached vs just checkboxes.
  • Limited transparency: leaders cannot easily answer “Where are we exposed?” or “What changed since last quarter?”.

Governance teams need a repeatable decision system, not another static report: something that turns policies, inventories, incidents, and evidence into workflows they can run every time a new AI system comes online.

Goal

Prototype an AI governance workbench that a real governance lead could recognize: one place to see systems and KPIs, summarize and audit policies, attach evidence, and ask grounded questions about risk and compliance.

Solution Architecture, Data Model & Applications

1. How the workbench is designed

The workbench treats AI governance as a decision system rather than a static set of documents. All core concepts (systems, policies, controls, evidence, incidents) are represented in simple tables and surfaced through a small number of high‑value workflows in Streamlit.

Instead of building a single “mega dashboard”, the design favors modular views that match how governance teams actually work day‑to‑day: monitor KPIs, explore data, review policies, run audits, and answer ad‑hoc questions.

  • SQL + tables as the backbone, so changes are transparent and reviewable.
  • Streamlit modules as the UX layer, giving non‑technical users a clean way to interact with governance data.
  • Gemini + RAG as assistive tooling, confined to summarization and Q&A grounded in stored documents.

2. Data Landscape & Event Model

Governance information is modeled as a set of tidy tables that reflect how AI systems, policies, controls, and evidence flow through a typical governance function. The event model is simple but extensible: each entity has a clear owner, lifecycle state, and linkage to related entities (e.g., a control links to a policy section and to one or more evidence artifacts).

Core entities and their roles:

  • Systems: AI/ML systems in scope (id, owner, domain, risk tier). Each system can be linked to multiple policies and controls.
  • Policies & Controls: documents, sections, and mapped control requirements (e.g., EU AI Act Article 9, NIST AI RMF subcategories). Controls are the atomic units of compliance that evidence must satisfy.
  • Evidence: links to model cards, assessments, test reports, and sign‑offs. Each evidence record points to one or more controls and optionally to a system.
  • Incidents & exceptions: timestamps, severity, status, owning team. These feed into KPIs such as incident aging and open count by domain.
  • Usage logs: how the workbench itself is used (summaries generated, audits run, Q&A questions). Useful for adoption and improvement.

In this portfolio version, these tables are populated from CSV and synthetic data, but the structure mirrors what a real governance function would expect from a model registry and incident system. The schema is designed so that new frameworks (e.g., sector‑specific guidelines) can be added without changing the core pipeline.

This architecture keeps the LLM layer clearly separated from system of record data: the assistant reads from curated tables and documents, rather than directly querying production systems. That separation is critical for auditability and for preventing the model from “hallucinating” governance state.

3. Metrics, KPIs & North Star

The workbench is built around a small set of governance KPIs that answer “how healthy is our AI program?” rather than “how accurate is model X?”. The north star is evidence coverage: the share of in‑scope controls that have at least one concrete, up‑to‑date evidence artifact. Secondary KPIs include:

  • Incident aging: distribution of open incidents by days open; goal is to surface stale or forgotten items.
  • Overdue reviews: count of systems or controls past their scheduled review date.
  • Systems in scope: total and by risk tier (e.g., high‑risk vs limited‑risk under EU AI Act).
  • Review velocity: time from “review requested” to “review completed” for sampled items.

These metrics are exposed on the Governance Dashboard so that leadership can see trends over time and drill down by domain, owner, or framework. The design choice to treat governance KPIs as first‑class (rather than burying them in spreadsheets) is what makes the workbench feel like a control room instead of a document store.

4. Analytical & Experimental Approach

The workbench is not a single monolithic app but a set of modules that can be run independently or in sequence. Data flows from raw CSVs (or, in production, from a model registry and incident system) into staging and feature tables; Streamlit pages then query these tables and, where needed, call the Gemini API for summarization and Q&A. The experimental approach for the RAG assistant was iterative: start with naive chunking, add metadata (system, control type), tune chunk size and overlap, and evaluate on a small set of “golden” questions before rolling out to users.

No formal A/B tests were run in this portfolio version, but the design supports them: e.g., comparing audit engine rule sets or RAG prompts across different policy documents to see which yields more actionable reports.

5. AI & Automation Layer

The workbench uses AI in three places, each scoped to reduce risk and keep humans in the loop:

  • Policy summarization: Gemini is used to generate short summaries and bullet points from uploaded policy PDFs/DOCX. Output is reviewed before being stored or turned into flashcards.
  • Compliance audit prototype: a rule‑based (plus optional LLM‑assisted) scanner looks for phrases and concepts (e.g., “data lineage”, “human oversight”) and produces a gap report. The final “pass/fail” or “needs review” decision remains with the user.
  • RAG Q&A assistant: users ask questions in natural language; the system retrieves relevant chunks from uploaded documents and optional metadata, then Gemini generates an answer grounded in those chunks. Citations are shown so users can verify.

RAG Engine Implementation (Python)

This code block demonstrates how the Gemini-backed Q&A assistant chunks documents, generates embeddings, and retrieves relevant chunks with context-grounding: 

import google.generativeai as genai
from langchain_text_splitters import RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter
from langchain_community.vectorstores import FAISS
from langchain_google_genai import GoogleGenAIEmbeddings

# Initialize text splitter & embeddings
text_splitter = RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter(chunk_size=1000, chunk_overlap=150)
embeddings = GoogleGenAIEmbeddings(model="models/embedding-001")

def build_vector_store(documents):
    """Split documents and build FAISS vector index."""
    chunks = text_splitter.split_documents(documents)
    vector_store = FAISS.from_documents(chunks, embeddings)
    return vector_store

def query_governance_assistant(query, vector_store):
    """Retrieve relevant chunks and query Gemini with context grounding."""
    # Retrieve top 4 relevant chunks
    docs = vector_store.similarity_search(query, k=4)
    context = "\\n\\n".join([doc.page_content for doc in docs])
    
    # Ground the prompt with retrieved policy texts
    prompt = f"""
    You are an expert AI Governance Advisor. Answer the question using ONLY the provided policy context below.
    If the context does not contain the answer, say "I cannot find the answer in the provided documents."
    
    Context:
    {{context}}
    
    Question: {{query}}
    Answer with page citations where possible:
    """
    
    model = genai.GenerativeModel('gemini-1.5-flash')
    response = model.generate_content(prompt)
    return response.text, docs

Automation is limited to repetitive, high‑volume tasks (summarization, scanning). Approval workflows, sign‑offs, and policy decisions are not automated; the workbench surfaces information to speed up human judgement, not replace it.

6. Applications & Workbench Modules

Governance Dashboard

A high‑level view of systems in scope, open incidents, overdue reviews, and other KPIs that AI governance leaders track. Heatmaps and time‑series views show incident aging and exceptions by domain or tier.

Data Visualizer

A flexible visual layer for governance datasets (inventories, incidents, assessments). Users can upload CSV/JSON/XLSX or use sample data, then build charts to explore trends and outliers without leaving the workbench.

Policy Summarizer & Flashcards

Upload policy PDFs or DOCX files, generate structured summaries, and create flashcards to quickly onboard reviewers to new frameworks or internal guidelines.

Compliance Audit Prototype

A simple audit engine scans documents for indicators like data lineage, human oversight, or fairness, and produces a markdown‑style report that highlights strengths and gaps.

Gemini + RAG Assistant

A Q&A interface where users can ask “Where do we talk about human oversight?” or “Which systems are impacted by X regulation?” and get answers grounded in uploaded documents and structured metadata—not model guesses alone.

Analysis, Insights & Business Impact

7. Observations & Key Findings

  • Governance can be productized.
    Observation: turning static documents into interactive modules (dashboards, summarizer, audits) makes governance feel like a workflow, not a one‑off review.
    Why it matters: productized workflows scale better across many AI systems and reduce dependency on a few experts.
  • LLMs add the most value in the last mile.
    Observation: Gemini and RAG work best when they summarize, prioritize, or explain; judgement stays with humans.
    Why it matters: treating LLMs as assistive tools lowers risk while still improving reviewer productivity.
  • Information design is critical for RAG.
    Observation: adding metadata (system, domain, control type) and curated chunks dramatically improves answer quality.
    Why it matters: good retrieval design is as important as model choice when building governance assistants.
  • Governance KPIs need equal footing with model metrics.
    Observation: incident aging, overdue reviews, and evidence coverage are as critical as precision/recall.
    Why it matters: this reframes success from “is the model good?” to “is the AI program governed well?”.

8. Trade-offs, Risks & Failure Modes

The workbench makes deliberate trade-offs and has known failure modes that governance teams should be aware of:

  • RAG hallucination: if retrieval returns weak or irrelevant chunks, the LLM may still produce a plausible-sounding answer. Mitigation: show citations, require human verification for high-stakes questions, and tune chunking and metadata.
  • Audit engine oversimplification: the prototype uses keyword and concept matching; it can miss nuance or misclassify a control as “covered” when evidence is weak. Mitigation: treat audit output as a draft; final assessment stays with reviewers.
  • Scale and latency: with many documents and large chunks, RAG latency can grow. Mitigation: limit document set per session, use smaller models or distilled embeddings where appropriate, and cache frequent queries.
  • Bias and fairness: summarization and Q&A inherit model biases. Mitigation: use models with documented safety profiles, review sample outputs, and avoid automating final policy or fairness decisions.

9. Results & Business Impact (Indicative)

Even with synthetic and demo data, the workbench mirrors impact levers that real AI governance leaders care about:

  • Faster reviews: centralizing KPIs, documents, and audits reduces time-to-review for AI systems. Instead of spending days gathering documents, reading policies, and checking evidence, reviewers can use the workbench to get a complete picture in hours. The Governance Dashboard shows at a glance which systems are overdue, which controls have gaps, and which incidents need attention, eliminating the need to query multiple systems or email threads.
  • Better evidence coverage: mapping controls to concrete artifacts clarifies where obligations are under-served. The workbench shows evidence coverage as a percentage (e.g., "45% of controls have evidence attached"), making it easy to see which systems or frameworks need more documentation. This helps governance teams prioritize evidence collection and reduces the risk of compliance gaps going unnoticed until audits.
  • Shared understanding: dashboards, summaries, and Q&A give legal, risk, and engineering a common view of AI risk. Instead of each team working from different documents or spreadsheets, everyone sees the same KPIs, summaries, and audit results. This reduces misalignment and speeds up decision-making (e.g., "should we approve this AI system for production?").
  • Auditability: explicit tables and logs make it easier to show what was reviewed and when. When regulators or auditors ask "how do you govern AI systems?", the workbench provides a clear, documented process with timestamps, evidence links, and review outcomes. This is critical for compliance with regulations like the EU AI Act, which require documented governance processes.
  • Scalability: the workbench can handle many AI systems (hundreds or thousands) without requiring proportional increases in governance headcount. By automating repetitive tasks (summarization, scanning) and surfacing high-priority items (overdue reviews, evidence gaps), the workbench enables small governance teams to oversee large AI portfolios.

10. Recommendations for Governance Teams

  • Start with a clear system inventory and control map before introducing complex tooling.
  • Define and track a small set of governance KPIs (incident aging, overdue reviews, evidence coverage).
  • Use LLMs for summaries and explanations, not for final policy decisions.
  • Embed governance workflows into day‑to‑day work, not just annual reviews or audits.

11. Limitations & What We'd Improve

This workbench is a portfolio prototype. Known limitations and improvements we would make in a production setting:

  • Data: tables are CSV/synthetic; production would connect to a real model registry, incident system, and document store.
  • RAG: chunking and retrieval could be tuned per document type; multi‑hop and follow‑up questions are not fully supported.
  • Audit engine: rule sets are hardcoded; a configurable, framework‑specific rule engine would improve reuse.
  • Access control: no authentication or role‑based views; multi‑tenant and compliance‑sensitive deployments would require both.

12. Future Scope (12–24 Months)

  • Integrate with a real model registry and incident system instead of CSV‑backed tables.
  • Add multi‑user workflows with authentication, roles, and review assignments.
  • Extend the audit engine with configurable rule sets per framework (EU AI Act, NIST AI RMF, internal standards).
  • Introduce trend analysis and alerting for governance KPIs (e.g., spikes in incidents or overdue reviews).
  • Support versioned policy documents and diff views so “what changed” is visible between review cycles.

Conclusion

The AI Governance Workbench demonstrates that governance can be productized: static policies and scattered evidence can be turned into a repeatable workflow with clear KPIs, dashboards, and assistive AI. By keeping the LLM in the “last mile” (summaries, Q&A, draft audits) and leaving decisions to humans, the design reduces risk while improving speed and clarity. For organizations scaling AI/LLM deployments, a workbench like this can materially reduce time‑to‑review and improve evidence coverage, giving leaders a clearer, auditable view of AI risk without hiring a large new team.
