Project Overview

The AI Governance Workbench is a SQL‑ and Streamlit‑first control room for AI governance teams: it combines risk dashboards, document summarization, compliance audits, and a Gemini + RAG assistant into one place so policy owners can see AI system health and answer “are we covered?” in minutes instead of weeks.

Executive Summary

  • Business problem: AI/LLM deployments move fast, but governance still runs on PDFs, spreadsheets, and email threads, making it hard to see what is in scope, which controls are missing, and who owns the risk.
  • What was built: a multi‑module Streamlit workbench with governance KPIs, a data visualizer, policy summarizer + flashcards, a compliance audit prototype, and a Gemini‑backed Q&A assistant grounded in uploaded documents.
  • Why it matters operationally: turns static AI policies into a repeatable workflow for reviews, evidence collection, and go/no‑go decisions across many systems.
  • Scale & realism: simulated but structured tables for systems, policies, controls, evidence, and incidents that mirror what real governance teams track.
  • Key insights: governance can be productized, LLMs are most useful in the “last mile” (summaries, explanations), and KPIs like incident aging and evidence coverage are as important as traditional model metrics.
  • Indicative impact: a workbench like this can materially reduce time‑to‑review and improve evidence coverage, giving leaders a clearer, auditable view of AI risk without hiring a large new team.

Business Problem: Fragmented AI Governance Workflows

Most organizations now run pilots or production workloads with AI and LLMs, but governance has not kept up. Policies live in PDFs on shared drives, model cards sit in separate tools, and incident logs and evidence are scattered across email threads, tickets, and spreadsheets.

  • No single view of AI systems in scope for a given policy, framework, or regulation.
  • Manual, slow reviews where teams read long documents and try to remember what was checked last time.
  • Poor evidence coverage: it is hard to see which controls have concrete artifacts attached vs just checkboxes.
  • Limited transparency: leaders cannot easily answer “Where are we exposed?” or “What changed since last quarter?”.

Governance teams need a repeatable decision system, not another static report: something that turns policies, inventories, incidents, and evidence into workflows they can run every time a new AI system comes online.

Goal

Prototype an AI governance workbench that a real governance lead could recognize: one place to see systems and KPIs, summarize and audit policies, attach evidence, and ask grounded questions about risk and compliance.

Solution Architecture, Data Model & Applications

1. How the workbench is designed

The workbench treats AI governance as a decision system rather than a static set of documents. All core concepts (systems, policies, controls, evidence, incidents) are represented in simple tables and surfaced through a small number of high‑value workflows in Streamlit.

Instead of building a single “mega dashboard”, the design favors modular views that match how governance teams actually work day‑to‑day: monitor KPIs, explore data, review policies, run audits, and answer ad‑hoc questions.

  • SQL + tables as the backbone, so changes are transparent and reviewable.
  • Streamlit modules as the UX layer, giving non‑technical users a clean way to interact with governance data.
  • Gemini + RAG as assistive tooling, confined to summarization and Q&A grounded in stored documents.

2. Data & System Design

Governance information is modeled as a set of tidy tables:

  • Systems: AI/ML systems in scope (id, owner, domain, risk tier).
  • Policies & Controls: documents, sections, mapped control requirements.
  • Evidence: links to model cards, assessments, tests, and sign‑offs.
  • Incidents & exceptions: timestamps, severity, status, owning team.
  • Usage logs: how the workbench itself is used (summaries, audits, questions).

In this portfolio version, these tables are populated from CSV and synthetic data, but the structure mirrors what a real governance function would expect from a model registry and incident system.

3. Applications & Workbench Modules

Governance Dashboard

A high‑level view of systems in scope, open incidents, overdue reviews, and other KPIs that AI governance leaders track. Heatmaps and time‑series views show incident aging and exceptions by domain or tier.

Data Visualizer

A flexible visual layer for governance datasets (inventories, incidents, assessments). Users can upload CSV/JSON/XLSX or use sample data, then build charts to explore trends and outliers without leaving the workbench.

Policy Summarizer & Flashcards

Upload policy PDFs or DOCX files, generate structured summaries, and create flashcards to quickly onboard reviewers to new frameworks or internal guidelines.

Compliance Audit Prototype

A simple audit engine scans documents for indicators like data lineage, human oversight, or fairness, and produces a markdown‑style report that highlights strengths and gaps.

Gemini + RAG Assistant

A Q&A interface where users can ask “Where do we talk about human oversight?” or “Which systems are impacted by X regulation?” and get answers grounded in uploaded documents and structured metadata—not model guesses alone.

Analysis, Insights & Business Impact

4. Analysis & Key Insights

  • Governance can be productized.
    Observation: turning static documents into interactive modules (dashboards, summarizer, audits) makes governance feel like a workflow, not a one‑off review.
    Why it matters: productized workflows scale better across many AI systems and reduce dependency on a few experts.
  • LLMs add the most value in the last mile.
    Observation: Gemini and RAG work best when they summarize, prioritize, or explain; judgement stays with humans.
    Why it matters: treating LLMs as assistive tools lowers risk while still improving reviewer productivity.
  • Information design is critical for RAG.
    Observation: adding metadata (system, domain, control type) and curated chunks dramatically improves answer quality.
    Why it matters: good retrieval design is as important as model choice when building governance assistants.
  • Governance KPIs need equal footing with model metrics.
    Observation: incident aging, overdue reviews, and evidence coverage are as critical as precision/recall.
    Why it matters: this reframes success from “is the model good?” to “is the AI program governed well?”.

5. Results & Business Impact (Indicative)

Even with synthetic and demo data, the workbench mirrors impact levers that real AI governance leaders care about:

  • Faster reviews: centralizing KPIs, documents, and audits reduces time-to-review for AI systems.
  • Better evidence coverage: mapping controls to concrete artifacts clarifies where obligations are under-served.
  • Shared understanding: dashboards, summaries, and Q&A give legal, risk, and engineering a common view of AI risk.
  • Auditability: explicit tables and logs make it easier to show what was reviewed and when.

6. Recommendations for Governance Teams

  • Start with a clear system inventory and control map before introducing complex tooling.
  • Define and track a small set of governance KPIs (incident aging, overdue reviews, evidence coverage).
  • Use LLMs for summaries and explanations, not for final policy decisions.
  • Embed governance workflows into day‑to‑day work, not just annual reviews or audits.

7. Future Work

  • Integrate with a real model registry and incident system instead of CSV‑backed tables.
  • Add multi‑user workflows with authentication, roles, and review assignments.
  • Extend the audit engine with configurable rule sets per framework (EU AI Act, NIST AI RMF, internal standards).
  • Introduce trend analysis and alerting for governance KPIs (e.g., spikes in incidents or overdue reviews).
