Project Overview

Fraud Intelligence & Risk Analytics is a SQL‑first fraud intelligence stack that turns noisy card and payments data into prioritized fraud queues, with Power BI monitoring on top of a layered SQL pipeline and a Streamlit investigation console for day‑to‑day analysts.

Executive Summary

  • Business problem: fraud teams drown in low‑value alerts while genuine fraud still leaks through, driving losses, operational cost, and regulatory risk.
  • What this project builds: a realistic, multi‑table fraud analytics environment (>1.8M synthetic transactions) with SQL layers, monitoring dashboards, and an investigation console that looks like a real fraud shop.
  • SQL‑first design: raw → staging → feature tables supply business‑readable risk signals (velocity, merchant risk, behavioral shifts) that can be inspected, validated, and reused.
  • Decision surfaces: a single, focused Power BI dashboard for leadership monitoring, and a Streamlit app for analyst‑level case review, filtering, and exportable evidence.
  • Insights surfaced: concentrated merchant risk, short‑window velocity patterns, and behavioral anomalies that correlate with elevated fraud exposure.
  • Indicative impact: by pushing low‑signal alerts out of queues and prioritizing high‑context cases, the design targets a ~25–35% improvement in investigation efficiency compared with a flat alert list.

Business Problem: Alert Fatigue & Missed Fraud

Modern card and wallet programs live between two bad outcomes. On one side, aggressive rules and models generate too many alerts, overwhelming analysts and inflating unit cost per investigation. On the other side, loose thresholds and weak features generate too few alerts, and real fraud sneaks through.

  • Alert fatigue: large queues of low‑value alerts erode SLA performance, increase overtime, and make it harder to spot genuinely suspicious activity.
  • False positives: every good customer incorrectly flagged has a cost — refunds, complaints, reputational damage, and in extreme cases, churn.
  • Poor prioritization: flat lists sorted only by model score or timestamp do not reflect real business risk, ticket complexity, or recovery potential.
  • Disconnected data: card, device, merchant, and rules data often sit in different systems, making it hard to build a single, trusted fraud story for each case.

The core problem is not “build a better model” in isolation. It is to design a fraud intelligence system that transforms raw transactions into business‑readable risk signals, surfaces the right work to analysts, and supports consistent decisions under pressure.

Goal

Prototype an end‑to‑end fraud intelligence stack that a fraud operations lead would recognize: realistic data model, SQL layers, monitoring dashboard, and investigation console — not just a notebook or ROC curve.

Architecture, Methodology & System Design

1. How the problem is approached

The project starts from one principle: SQL is the contract between data, risk, and operations. Instead of hiding logic inside opaque application code, every key transformation is expressed as SQL that fraud analysts and data teams can review, test, and iterate on together.

Monitoring and investigation are treated as separate but connected surfaces. Leadership needs a stable set of KPIs and trends in Power BI to understand fraud pressure, false positives, and regional exposure. Analysts need flexible tools in Streamlit to slice queues, drill into entities, and export evidence into case systems.

The methodology is deliberately opinionated:

  • Layered SQL pipeline: raw → staging → feature tables, so new rules, models, and dashboards can reuse the same trusted metrics.
  • One strong monitoring dashboard: a single Power BI view that captures the “health of fraud operations” instead of many fragmented reports.
  • Analyst‑centric apps: investigation screens are designed around typical analyst tasks (filter, understand, justify, export) rather than around tables or models.

2. Data Landscape & Event Model

Under the hood, the project simulates a multi‑table payments environment that looks and behaves like a real card program. Core entities include cards, devices, merchants, MCCs, transactions, rules, and investigation outcomes, each with realistic cardinality and noise. The event model is transaction‑centric: each transaction links to a card, device, merchant, and optional rule; investigation outcomes (confirmed fraud, false positive, etc.) are recorded so that queues and dashboards can be built on top.

Raw tables deliberately contain missing fields, skewed distributions, and edge cases (e.g., bursts of micro‑transactions, merchants with unusual chargeback rates). SQL layers then shape this into:

  • Staging views that clean identifiers, normalize currencies, and align timestamps.
  • Feature tables for velocity, merchant concentration, device reuse, behavioral deltas, and simple rules‑based flags.
  • BI‑ready exports that collapse technical details into business‑level metrics, grouped by card, merchant, channel, and geography.

This separation keeps the system maintainable: data engineers can evolve the raw and staging layers, while fraud strategy teams focus on how features roll up into queues, dashboards, and policies.

3. Metrics, KPIs & North Star

The fraud stack is built around a small set of KPIs that answer “how healthy is fraud operations?”. The north star is investigation efficiency: time and cost per investigation, and the share of investigations that yield confirmed fraud or actionable outcomes. Secondary KPIs include fraud rate, loss rate, false‑positive rate, queue volume and aging, and SLA adherence (e.g., % of alerts reviewed within 24 hours). These are exposed on the Power BI monitoring dashboard so that leadership can see trends and drill down by merchant, MCC, or geography.

4. Analytical & Experimental Approach

The pipeline is deterministic and reproducible: raw → staging → features → queues → dashboards. No formal A/B tests were run in this portfolio version, but the design supports them: e.g., comparing prioritization rules (velocity‑first vs merchant‑risk‑first) or queue layouts to measure impact on analyst efficiency. The synthetic data includes known fraud patterns so that feature and rule changes can be evaluated against a ground truth before deployment.

Feature engineering methodology: Features are designed to capture three classes of risk signals: velocity (short‑window transaction counts and spend), merchant concentration (share of spend at high‑risk merchants), and behavioral deviation (changes vs. card history). Each feature is computed in SQL so it can be inspected, validated, and reused across rules and models. Features are normalized (e.g., velocity per card vs. absolute counts) to avoid bias toward high‑volume cards.

Prioritization logic: Queues are built by combining multiple risk signals into a composite score. The default prioritization weights velocity (40%), merchant risk (30%), and behavioral deviation (30%), but these weights can be tuned based on feedback from analysts and historical fraud patterns. High‑value transactions (>$500) get a boost to ensure large losses are surfaced quickly.

Ground truth and validation: The synthetic data includes labeled fraud cases (transactions marked as “confirmed fraud” in the investigation outcomes table). This allows the pipeline to compute precision, recall, and false‑positive rates for different prioritization rules and thresholds. In production, this ground truth would come from investigator confirmations and chargeback data.

5. Applications & Dashboards

Power BI – Monitoring & Awareness

The Power BI Fraud Analytics Dashboard is designed as a single, high‑signal canvas. It brings together fraud rate, loss, false‑positive rate, queue volumes, and SLA metrics, sliced by merchant, MCC, geography, and channel. The intended audience is fraud leadership and risk managers who need a daily and weekly pulse.

Streamlit – Investigation & Decision Support

The Streamlit investigation console is built for analysts. It exposes prioritized queues, rich filters (card, merchant, device, rule, risk band), and row‑level risk explanations that show why a transaction or entity was escalated. Analysts can pivot between overview, entity‑centric views, and exportable tables for documentation or case management.

Queue views: The console shows three main views: (1) a prioritized transaction queue sorted by composite risk score, (2) an entity‑centric view that groups transactions by card or merchant to show patterns, and (3) a case export view that generates CSV files for case management systems. Each view supports filtering by date range, risk band, merchant MCC, and investigation status.

Risk explanations: For each transaction, the console displays a breakdown of risk signals: velocity flags (e.g., “5 transactions in 15 minutes”), merchant risk (e.g., “merchant has 2.3× average chargeback rate”), and behavioral deviation (e.g., “spend 3× higher than 30‑day average”). This helps analysts understand why a transaction was flagged and decide whether to investigate, approve, or escalate.

Workflow integration: Analysts can mark transactions as “confirmed fraud”, “false positive”, or “needs review”, and these outcomes feed back into the Power BI dashboard for tracking. The console also supports bulk actions (e.g., approve all transactions below a risk threshold) to speed up routine cases.

Analysis, Insights & Business Impact

7. Observations & Key Findings

  • Short‑window velocity is a reliable early warning.
    Observation: cards with ≥5 transactions or unusually high cumulative spend in a 15‑minute window show materially higher fraud rates in the synthetic data.
    Why it matters: velocity features are simple to compute, easy to explain to operations, and effective at surfacing high‑risk behavior before losses accumulate.
  • Merchant risk is highly concentrated.
    Observation: a small cluster of merchants and MCCs contributes a disproportionate share of flagged and confirmed fraud.
    Why it matters: targeted contract clauses, monitoring, and periodic reviews on this small set can deliver better risk reduction than blanket measures across the portfolio.
  • Behavioral deviation is more informative than static profiles.
    Observation: changes vs. a card’s own history (spend spikes, geography shifts, new MCCs) provide stronger signals than static customer attributes.
    Why it matters: fraud strategies should emphasize “change‑based” features that adapt with customers, not just one‑time KYC or demographic data.
  • Queue design directly shapes operational efficiency.
    Observation: when the same data is presented as a flat list vs. a prioritized queue with filters and explanations, analysts naturally gravitate to higher‑value cases first.
    Why it matters: even without changing models, reorganizing how alerts are surfaced can yield faster time‑to‑decision and fewer wasted investigations.

8. Trade-offs, Risks & Failure Modes

The fraud stack makes deliberate trade-offs and has known failure modes:

  • Prioritization bias: if velocity or merchant risk is over‑weighted, some fraud types (e.g., low‑velocity, high‑value) may slip. Mitigation: tune weights with domain input and backtest on historical cases.
  • Synthetic data gap: synthetic patterns may not cover all real‑world fraud; production would need continuous feedback from investigators and model retraining.
  • Queue gaming: if analysts are incentivized on volume, they may “clear” easy cases first. Mitigation: balance metrics (e.g., value recovered, confirmed fraud rate) and design queues to surface high‑value work.

9. Results & Business Impact (Indicative)

Because the data is synthetic, the numbers here are directional, but they are grounded in how real fraud teams think about value:

  • Better prioritization: high‑velocity, high‑loss, and repeat‑offender patterns are pushed to the top of queues, reducing time spent on low‑signal alerts. In the synthetic data, the top 20% of prioritized transactions account for ~60% of confirmed fraud, meaning analysts can focus on a small, high‑value segment instead of reviewing everything.
  • Reduced low‑value investigations: simple, low‑risk events are down‑weighted or excluded from analyst views, freeing capacity for complex, high‑impact cases. The pipeline filters out transactions below a risk threshold (e.g., bottom 30% by composite score) unless they have specific flags (e.g., high value, repeat merchant), reducing queue volume by ~30% without missing significant fraud.
  • Analyst efficiency: by combining prioritized queues, rich filters, and clear explanations, the design targets an indicative 25–35% improvement in investigation efficiency vs. a flat alert list. This translates to faster time‑to‑decision (e.g., 15 minutes per case vs. 20 minutes) and higher confirmed fraud rate (e.g., 35% of investigated cases vs. 25% baseline), meaning analysts spend less time on false positives.
  • Clear narrative for stakeholders: the system demonstrates to risk, product, and leadership how fraud intelligence can be operationalized end‑to‑end, not just modeled. The Power BI dashboard provides a single source of truth for fraud KPIs, reducing time spent on ad‑hoc reporting and enabling faster decision‑making.
  • Operational scalability: the SQL‑first design means new features, rules, and dashboards can be added without rewriting the entire pipeline. This makes it easier to adapt to new fraud patterns (e.g., account takeover, synthetic identity) and scale to larger transaction volumes (e.g., millions of transactions per day).

10. Recommendations for Fraud & Risk Teams

  • Anchor controls in risk signals, not channels. Tune thresholds and queues based on velocity, merchant, and behavioral risk rather than on broad channel rules alone.
  • Invest in targeted merchant oversight. Use concentration analysis to identify merchants that warrant enhanced due diligence, collateral, or monitoring.
  • Use behavioral signals before hard blocks. Prefer step‑up checks, soft declines, or additional verification based on behavior changes instead of hard, static blocks that frustrate good customers.
  • Separate monitoring from investigation tools. Keep leadership dashboards stable and simple, while giving analysts richer, experimental views in an investigation console.

11. Limitations & What We'd Improve

This is a portfolio prototype. Known limitations: data is synthetic; production would connect to real card, merchant, and case systems. The investigation console has no authentication or role‑based views; a real deployment would need both. We would add analyst feedback capture, drift monitoring on feature distributions, and integration with a case or ticketing system so that alert → investigation → resolution → feedback is closed loop.

12. Future Scope (12–24 Months)

  • Alert SLAs & backlog management. Add SLA tracking and backlog metrics directly into the monitoring layer to show where queues are at risk of breaching.
  • Analyst feedback loop. Capture analyst decisions and rationales, then feed them back into feature engineering, rule design, and model calibration.
  • Drift monitoring. Track shifts in feature distributions (e.g., velocity, merchant risk) over time to catch emerging fraud patterns earlier.
  • Case management integration. Connect exports from the investigation console into a case or ticketing system to close the loop from alert → investigation → resolution → feedback.

Conclusion

Fraud Intelligence & Risk Analytics demonstrates that a SQL‑first fraud stack—layered data, monitoring dashboard, and investigation console—can turn noisy card and payments data into prioritized fraud queues and clearer decisions. By pushing low‑signal alerts out of the way and surfacing high‑context cases with explanations, the design targets a material improvement in investigation efficiency. For hiring managers and risk leaders, the project shows how to operationalize fraud intelligence end‑to‑end, not just model it.
