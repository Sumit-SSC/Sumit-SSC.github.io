Project Overview

Fraud Intelligence & Risk Analytics is a SQL‑first fraud intelligence stack that turns noisy card and payments data into prioritized fraud queues, with Power BI monitoring on top of a layered SQL pipeline and a Streamlit investigation console for day‑to‑day analysts.

Executive Summary

  • Business problem: fraud teams drown in low‑value alerts while genuine fraud still leaks through, driving losses, operational cost, and regulatory risk.
  • What this project builds: a realistic, multi‑table fraud analytics environment (>1.8M synthetic transactions) with SQL layers, monitoring dashboards, and an investigation console that looks like a real fraud shop.
  • SQL‑first design: raw → staging → feature tables supply business‑readable risk signals (velocity, merchant risk, behavioral shifts) that can be inspected, validated, and reused.
  • Decision surfaces: a single, focused Power BI dashboard for leadership monitoring, and a Streamlit app for analyst‑level case review, filtering, and exportable evidence.
  • Insights surfaced: concentrated merchant risk, short‑window velocity patterns, and behavioral anomalies that correlate with elevated fraud exposure.
  • Indicative impact: by pushing low‑signal alerts out of queues and prioritizing high‑context cases, the design targets a ~25–35% improvement in investigation efficiency compared with a flat alert list.

Business Problem: Alert Fatigue & Missed Fraud

Modern card and wallet programs live between two bad outcomes. On one side, aggressive rules and models generate too many alerts, overwhelming analysts and inflating unit cost per investigation. On the other side, loose thresholds and weak features generate too few alerts, and real fraud sneaks through.

  • Alert fatigue: large queues of low‑value alerts erode SLA performance, increase overtime, and make it harder to spot genuinely suspicious activity.
  • False positives: every good customer incorrectly flagged has a cost — refunds, complaints, reputational damage, and in extreme cases, churn.
  • Poor prioritization: flat lists sorted only by model score or timestamp do not reflect real business risk, ticket complexity, or recovery potential.
  • Disconnected data: card, device, merchant, and rules data often sit in different systems, making it hard to build a single, trusted fraud story for each case.

The core problem is not “build a better model” in isolation. It is to design a fraud intelligence system that transforms raw transactions into business‑readable risk signals, surfaces the right work to analysts, and supports consistent decisions under pressure.

Goal

Prototype an end‑to‑end fraud intelligence stack that a fraud operations lead would recognize: realistic data model, SQL layers, monitoring dashboard, and investigation console — not just a notebook or ROC curve.

Architecture, Methodology & System Design

1. How the problem is approached

The project starts from one principle: SQL is the contract between data, risk, and operations. Instead of hiding logic inside opaque application code, every key transformation is expressed as SQL that fraud analysts and data teams can review, test, and iterate on together.

Monitoring and investigation are treated as separate but connected surfaces. Leadership needs a stable set of KPIs and trends in Power BI to understand fraud pressure, false positives, and regional exposure. Analysts need flexible tools in Streamlit to slice queues, drill into entities, and export evidence into case systems.

The methodology is deliberately opinionated:

  • Layered SQL pipeline: raw → staging → feature tables, so new rules, models, and dashboards can reuse the same trusted metrics.
  • One strong monitoring dashboard: a single Power BI view that captures the “health of fraud operations” instead of many fragmented reports.
  • Analyst‑centric apps: investigation screens are designed around typical analyst tasks (filter, understand, justify, export) rather than around tables or models.

2. Data & System Design

Under the hood, the project simulates a multi‑table payments environment that looks and behaves like a real card program. Core entities include cards, devices, merchants, MCCs, transactions, rules, and investigation outcomes, each with realistic cardinality and noise.

Raw tables deliberately contain missing fields, skewed distributions, and edge cases (e.g., bursts of micro‑transactions, merchants with unusual chargeback rates). SQL layers then shape this into:

  • Staging views that clean identifiers, normalize currencies, and align timestamps.
  • Feature tables for velocity, merchant concentration, device reuse, behavioral deltas, and simple rules‑based flags.
  • BI‑ready exports that collapse technical details into business‑level metrics, grouped by card, merchant, channel, and geography.

Analysis, Insights & Business Impact

4. Analysis & Key Insights

  • Short‑window velocity is a reliable early warning.
    Observation: cards with ≥5 transactions or unusually high cumulative spend in a 15‑minute window show materially higher fraud rates in the synthetic data.
    Why it matters: velocity features are simple to compute, easy to explain to operations, and effective at surfacing high‑risk behavior before losses accumulate.
  • Merchant risk is highly concentrated.
    Observation: a small cluster of merchants and MCCs contributes a disproportionate share of flagged and confirmed fraud.
    Why it matters: targeted contract clauses, monitoring, and periodic reviews on this small set can deliver better risk reduction than blanket measures across the portfolio.
  • Behavioral deviation is more informative than static profiles.
    Observation: changes vs. a card’s own history (spend spikes, geography shifts, new MCCs) provide stronger signals than static customer attributes.
    Why it matters: fraud strategies should emphasize “change‑based” features that adapt with customers, not just one‑time KYC or demographic data.
  • Queue design directly shapes operational efficiency.
    Observation: when the same data is presented as a flat list vs. a prioritized queue with filters and explanations, analysts naturally gravitate to higher‑value cases first.
    Why it matters: even without changing models, reorganizing how alerts are surfaced can yield faster time‑to‑decision and fewer wasted investigations.

5. Results & Business Impact (Indicative)

Because the data is synthetic, the numbers here are directional, but they are grounded in how real fraud teams think about value:

  • Better prioritization: high‑velocity, high‑loss, and repeat‑offender patterns are pushed to the top of queues, reducing time spent on low‑signal alerts.
  • Reduced low‑value investigations: simple, low‑risk events are down‑weighted or excluded from analyst views, freeing capacity for complex, high‑impact cases.
  • Analyst efficiency: by combining prioritized queues, rich filters, and clear explanations, the design targets an indicative 25–35% improvement in investigation efficiency vs. a flat alert list.
  • Clear narrative for stakeholders: the system demonstrates to risk, product, and leadership how fraud intelligence can be operationalized end‑to‑end, not just modeled.

6. Recommendations for Fraud & Risk Teams

  • Anchor controls in risk signals, not channels. Tune thresholds and queues based on velocity, merchant, and behavioral risk rather than on broad channel rules alone.
  • Invest in targeted merchant oversight. Use concentration analysis to identify merchants that warrant enhanced due diligence, collateral, or monitoring.
  • Use behavioral signals before hard blocks. Prefer step‑up checks, soft declines, or additional verification based on behavior changes instead of hard, static blocks that frustrate good customers.
  • Separate monitoring from investigation tools. Keep leadership dashboards stable and simple, while giving analysts richer, experimental views in an investigation console.

7. Future Work

  • Alert SLAs & backlog management. Add SLA tracking and backlog metrics directly into the monitoring layer to show where queues are at risk of breaching.
  • Analyst feedback loop. Capture analyst decisions and rationales, then feed them back into feature engineering, rule design, and model calibration.
  • Drift monitoring. Track shifts in feature distributions (e.g., velocity, merchant risk) over time to catch emerging fraud patterns earlier.
  • Case management integration. Connect exports from the investigation console into a case or ticketing system to close the loop from alert → investigation → resolution → feedback.
