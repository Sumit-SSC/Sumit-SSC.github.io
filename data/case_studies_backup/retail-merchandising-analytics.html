Retail merchandising and demand analytics sit at the heart of assortment planning, inventory turnover, and seasonal execution. This case study focuses on how retailers use sell-through, weeks of supply, and seasonality data—backed by SQL, BI, and Excel—to drive business decisions without heavy ML.

Executive Summary

Domain: Retail & merchandising. Buyers and planners need to know what sells, where, and when—and to align inventory with demand.

Data: POS/sell-through, inventory levels, assortment attributes (category, brand, price tier), and calendar (season, events).

Analytics focus: Sell-through rate, weeks of supply, sell-through by store/SKU/category, trend and seasonality, and assortment mix. Dashboards support replenishment and seasonal buy decisions.

Decision angle: What to reorder, what to mark down, and how to shape next season’s assortment using historical performance and forward-looking demand.

Why This Is a Business Analytics Problem

Merchandising teams live in spreadsheets and BI tools. The core questions—Did we run out? Did we overstock? Which categories over- or under-performed?—are answered with clear metrics and reports. SQL pipelines that aggregate POS and inventory, plus Power BI or Excel dashboards, deliver the single source of truth. Light forecasting (e.g. moving averages, seasonal indices) can support demand planning; the emphasis remains on descriptive and diagnostic analytics that drive action.

Key Metrics and Data Sources

  • Sell-through rate (units sold / units available) by SKU, store, and time window.
  • Weeks of supply and stock-out incidence—operational health of assortment.
  • Sell-through by category, brand, price tier—what drives revenue and margin.
  • Seasonality and calendar effects—holidays, events, weather—to plan buys and markdowns.
  • Assortment mix—breadth vs. depth, new vs. carryover—and performance by segment.

How Decisions Get Made

Replenishment and markdown decisions are driven by thresholds (e.g. weeks of supply < 2 = reorder; sell-through < 20% after 4 weeks = consider markdown). Assortment planning uses historical sell-through and margin by segment, plus qualitative inputs. Company reports (e.g. retail earnings calls, McKinsey/Deloitte retail analytics) and industry benchmarks inform target metrics. Analysts own the pipelines and dashboards; merchants own the decisions—clear handoff and definitions prevent confusion.
