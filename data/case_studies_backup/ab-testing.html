Product and growth teams run experiments to improve conversion and engagement; the analyst’s job is to ensure those experiments are designed and evaluated rigorously. This case study covers pre-test power, randomization checks, difference-in-proportions testing, and decision readouts—aligned with product analyst and decision scientist roles.

Executive Summary

Goal: Determine whether a product variant improves conversion without harming retention or revenue per user.

Approach: Pre-test power calculation; randomization checks; difference-in-proportions test; delta method CI; holdout check on retention; readout with ship/hold recommendation.

Insights: Variant lifted signup conversion by ~4.2 pp with 95% CI [1.1 pp, 7.3 pp]; recommended ship with monitoring on retention.

Outcome: Clear, reproducible readout that product and leadership could use for go/no-go decisions.

Business Context

A/B tests are only as good as their design and analysis. Underpowered tests lead to inconclusive results; poorly randomized tests can bias estimates. This project emphasized pre-experiment design, balance checks, and a single primary metric with a clear decision rule and follow-up monitoring plan.

Methodology

Design

Sample size was chosen via power analysis (e.g. 80% power to detect a 10% relative lift in conversion). Randomization was at user level; assignment was logged and validated for balance on key covariates.

Analysis

Randomization checks (e.g. chi-square, t-tests) confirmed balance. Primary metric was conversion (binary); difference-in-proportions z-test and delta method CI were used. Retention and revenue per user were checked as guardrail metrics; no material harm was detected.

Results & Readout

The variant showed a ~4.2 pp lift in signup conversion with 95% CI [1.1 pp, 7.3 pp]. The readout recommended ship with a monitoring plan for retention and revenue so that any delayed effects could be caught.

Future Work

  • CUPED or other variance reduction to improve sensitivity.
  • Segment-level analysis and heterogeneity (e.g. by cohort or region).
  • Automated readout templates and integration with experiment platforms.
