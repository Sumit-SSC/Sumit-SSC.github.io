Churn prediction isn't just about building accurate models—it's about turning risk scores into actionable retention strategies. This case study explores how I built an end-to-end churn analytics system that combines SQL feature engineering, ML risk scoring, and a retention strategy simulator to help subscription businesses optimize their retention spend and improve customer lifetime value.

Executive Summary

Dataset: 92,610 customers with realistic usage, billing, and support behavior. Synthetic data that mirrors real subscription businesses with usage patterns, payment history, support tickets, and lifecycle stages.

Models: Logistic Regression (baseline) + Random Forest (non-linear lift) with explainability. Both models provide risk scores and reason codes to guide interventions.

Performance: Random Forest achieves ROC AUC 0.96; Precision@Top 10% = 51%. This means 51% of the highest-risk customers actually churn, making the queue highly actionable for retention teams.

Decisioning: Retention simulator ties risk scores to ROI-based interventions. Teams can test different offer strategies (discounts, outreach, service credits) and see which segments deliver the best ROI.

Key outcome: Targeted interventions deliver ~2.3x ROI for high-risk segments in the 31-45 day intervention window, demonstrating how prediction + decisioning can improve retention efficiency.

The Business Context: Timing Matters in Retention

Subscription businesses care about when churn can be influenced. If you detect risk too late, the customer is already gone. If you detect it too early, you waste retention budget on customers who weren't actually going to churn. The sweet spot is the intervention window—typically 31-45 days before churn—when customers are still engaged enough to respond to offers but at-risk enough to benefit from intervention.

I've worked with retention teams that send generic offers to all at-risk customers, only to realize later that most of those customers wouldn't have churned anyway. The problem isn't lack of data—it's lack of precision. You need models that identify the highest-risk customers, explain why they're at risk, and help you choose the right intervention for each segment.

This project targets the 31-45 day intervention window and focuses on making churn risk actionable for retention teams. The system doesn't just predict churn—it helps teams decide who to target, what offer to make, and how much to spend. The retention simulator turns predictions into decisions, showing ROI for different strategies before teams commit budget.

The Core Challenge

How do you turn churn predictions into retention strategies that maximize ROI? The answer is combining accurate risk scoring with explainable features, segment-based targeting, and a simulator that lets teams test intervention strategies before deploying them.

Data & Feature Engineering: Building Predictive Signals

The strongest churn signals usually combine usage trajectory, payment friction, and support burden. The pipeline builds SQL feature tables that make those patterns explicit:

1. Usage Trend Features

Usage decline is one of the strongest churn predictors. I compute features that capture recent vs. historical usage patterns:

  • Usage decline ratio: Average usage in last 30 days vs. average usage in previous 30 days. Values < 0.8 indicate significant decline.
  • Days since last usage: How many days since the customer last used the product. Values > 7 days are concerning.
  • Usage volatility: Coefficient of variation in daily usage. High volatility can indicate disengagement.
  • Feature adoption: Number of product features used in last 30 days vs. historical average. Declining feature adoption signals churn risk.

Usage Trend SQL Example

WITH usage_trends AS (
  SELECT 
    customer_id,
    AVG(usage_minutes) FILTER (WHERE date >= CURRENT_DATE - 30) AS avg_usage_recent,
    AVG(usage_minutes) FILTER (WHERE date < CURRENT_DATE - 30 
                               AND date >= CURRENT_DATE - 60) AS avg_usage_historical,
    MAX(date) AS last_usage_date,
    STDDEV(usage_minutes) FILTER (WHERE date >= CURRENT_DATE - 30) AS usage_stddev_recent
  FROM staging.usage_daily
  GROUP BY customer_id
)
SELECT 
  customer_id,
  avg_usage_recent / NULLIF(avg_usage_historical, 0) AS usage_decline_ratio,
  CURRENT_DATE - last_usage_date AS days_since_last_usage,
  usage_stddev_recent / NULLIF(avg_usage_recent, 0) AS usage_volatility
FROM usage_trends

2. Billing & Payment Features

Payment friction is a strong churn signal. Customers with payment failures or invoice delays are at higher risk:

  • Payment failure count: Number of failed payment attempts in last 90 days. Values > 2 are concerning.
  • Invoice delay: Days between invoice date and payment date. Values > 14 days indicate payment friction.
  • Payment method age: How long the current payment method has been on file. New payment methods can indicate account changes.
  • Billing cycle changes: Customers who downgrade billing frequency (annual → monthly) show elevated churn risk.

3. Support & Engagement Features

Support burden and engagement patterns also predict churn:

  • Support ticket volume: Number of support tickets in last 90 days. High volume (> 3) can indicate frustration.
  • Unresolved issues: Number of open/unresolved support tickets. Values > 1 are concerning.
  • Support sentiment: Average sentiment score of support interactions (if available). Negative sentiment correlates with churn.
  • Email engagement: Open rate and click rate for marketing emails. Declining engagement signals disinterest.

4. Tenure & Lifecycle Features

Customer lifecycle stage and plan tier also matter:

  • Account age: Days since signup. Very new customers (< 30 days) and very old customers (> 2 years) have different risk profiles.
  • Plan tier: Free and Basic tiers show higher churn than Enterprise. This is a strong segmentation signal.
  • Upgrade/downgrade history: Customers who downgrade are at higher risk. Customers who upgrade are at lower risk.
  • Onboarding completion: Whether the customer completed onboarding steps. Incomplete onboarding correlates with early churn.

Modeling Approach: Interpretability + Performance

I use a two-model approach: Logistic Regression for interpretability and calibration, and Random Forest for non-linear lift. Both models provide risk scores and reason codes to guide interventions.

Model Architecture

  • Baseline: Logistic Regression - Provides interpretable coefficients and calibrated probabilities. Good for understanding feature importance and setting thresholds.
  • Non-linear lift: Random Forest - Captures interactions between features (e.g., usage decline + payment issues). Provides better discrimination but requires explainability techniques (SHAP values) to understand predictions.
  • Explainability: Both models rank drivers for each customer/segment. For Logistic Regression, coefficients show feature importance. For Random Forest, SHAP values show feature contributions to each prediction.

In a production setting, I'd add calibration (Platt scaling or Isotonic regression) to ensure predicted probabilities match actual churn rates, stability monitoring to detect model drift, and backtesting by cohort to validate performance over time.

Model Training Details

The Random Forest model uses:

  • 200 estimators (trees) for stability
  • Max depth of 8 to prevent overfitting
  • Min samples per leaf of 100 to ensure robust splits
  • Class weights balanced to handle class imbalance (churn is typically 5-15% of customers)
  • Random state for reproducibility

The model is trained on a 70/30 train/test split, with cross-validation to tune hyperparameters. Feature importance is computed using permutation importance and SHAP values for explainability.

Retention Strategy Simulator: Turning Predictions into Decisions

Models often fail in organizations because the next question is: "Okay, what do we do now?" The retention simulator turns predictions into decisions by letting teams test intervention strategies before deploying them.

How the Simulator Works

The simulator is a Streamlit app that lets retention teams:

  1. Select a risk segment: Choose from top 10%, top 25%, or custom risk threshold
  2. Choose an offer strategy: Discount (10%, 20%, 30%), outreach (email, phone, in-app), or service credits
  3. Set offer parameters: Discount amount, offer duration, eligibility criteria
  4. View ROI estimates: Expected retention lift, cost per customer, net ROI, and budget allocation
  5. Compare strategies: Side-by-side comparison of different offers to choose the best one

Simulator Logic Example

# For each customer in selected segment:
base_churn_prob = model.predict_proba(customer_features)[1]
offer_response_rate = estimate_response_rate(customer_segment, offer_type)
retention_lift = base_churn_prob * offer_response_rate * 0.3  # 30% of responders stay

# ROI calculation:
customer_lifetime_value = estimate_clv(customer_segment)
offer_cost = calculate_offer_cost(offer_type, offer_params)
net_roi = (retention_lift * customer_lifetime_value) - offer_cost

# Aggregate across segment:
segment_roi = sum(net_roi for all customers in segment)
segment_cost = sum(offer_cost for all customers in segment)
total_roi_ratio = segment_roi / segment_cost

Why the Simulator Matters

The simulator helps retention teams:

  • Test before deploying: See ROI estimates before committing budget, reducing wasted spend
  • Optimize offer strategy: Compare different offers to find the best one for each segment
  • Allocate budget efficiently: Focus retention spend on segments with highest ROI
  • Set expectations: Understand expected retention lift and costs before launching campaigns

Challenges & Lessons Learned

Challenge 1: Class Imbalance

Churn is typically 5-15% of customers, creating class imbalance. The solution is balanced class weights in Random Forest and careful threshold selection based on precision/recall trade-offs. For retention, precision matters more than recall—it's better to miss some churners than waste budget on false positives.

Challenge 2: Feature Drift

Customer behavior changes over time, causing feature distributions to drift. The solution is regular model retraining (monthly/quarterly) and monitoring feature distributions for drift. If features drift significantly, the model needs retraining.

Challenge 3: Intervention Window

The 31-45 day intervention window is a balance between early detection (enough time to intervene) and precision (customers are actually at risk). Too early, and you waste budget on false positives. Too late, and customers are already gone. The solution is segment-specific windows—new customers might need 60-day windows, while established customers might only need 30-day windows.

Future Work: Advanced Decision Science

1. Uplift Modeling

Instead of predicting churn risk, predict treatment effect—who will be saved by an offer vs. who would have stayed anyway. This requires randomized experiments to measure causal effects. Uplift models (e.g., Two-Model approach, Causal Forest) can identify "persuadables" who respond to offers, improving ROI by avoiding wasted spend on "sure things" and "lost causes."

2. Causal Evaluation

Use holdout groups and CUPED (Controlled-experiment Using Pre-Experiment Data) to improve measurement sensitivity. This allows detecting smaller treatment effects with the same sample size, or detecting the same effects with smaller samples. CUPED uses pre-experiment covariates to reduce variance in treatment effect estimates.

3. Policy Optimization

Use constrained optimization to allocate offers across segments, balancing:

  • Budget constraints: Total retention spend cannot exceed budget
  • Fairness constraints: Ensure offers are distributed fairly across segments
  • Segment SLAs: Minimum retention rates for key segments (e.g., Enterprise customers)
  • ROI maximization: Allocate budget to segments with highest ROI

This can be formulated as a linear programming problem or solved with gradient-based optimization.

4. Production Deployment

For production, I'd add:

  • Feature store: Centralized feature computation and serving (e.g., Feast, Tecton) to ensure consistent features across training and inference
  • Batch scoring: Score all customers daily/weekly and update risk scores in CRM or marketing automation tools
  • Model monitoring: Track model performance (precision, recall, AUC) and alert on degradation
  • Drift alerts: Monitor feature distributions and model predictions for drift, triggering retraining when needed
  • A/B testing framework: Test different models, thresholds, and offer strategies to continuously improve retention
