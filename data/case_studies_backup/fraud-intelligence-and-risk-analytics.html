Fraud detection isn't just about building a model—it's about building an investigation workflow that helps analysts prioritize cases, understand risk signals, and document decisions. This case study dives deep into how I designed a SQL-first fraud analytics platform that mirrors real-world payments operations, from raw transaction data to prioritized investigation queues and governance-friendly reporting.

Executive Summary

Goal: Turn high-volume transactions into a prioritized, explainable risk queue that improves analyst efficiency and reduces false positives. The system processes 1.8M+ transactions across cards, devices, merchants, rules, and investigator notes.

Approach: SQL-first staging and feature engineering, followed by risk scoring and analyst-facing UIs. The platform includes Power BI monitoring dashboards for daily oversight and Streamlit investigation screens for drill-down, case review, and export documentation.

Key outcome: Designed to target a 25-35% improvement in investigation efficiency by focusing analysts on the highest-value cases, with explainable risk reasons and audit-ready documentation.

Scale: 1.8M+ multi-table transactions with realistic payment patterns, device fingerprints, merchant relationships, and investigator feedback loops.

The Business Context: Why Fraud Operations Need Better Tools

In payments, fraud operations live and die by triage. If every "suspicious" rule triggers an alert, analysts drown in false positives. If detection is slow, losses and regulatory exposure grow. The challenge isn't just "can we detect fraud?" but "can we detect it early enough, prioritize it correctly, and document our decisions?"

I've worked with fraud teams that process thousands of alerts per day, with analysts spending hours on cases that turn out to be legitimate. The problem isn't lack of data—it's lack of signal. Raw transaction tables don't tell you "why this card is risky right now." You need velocity patterns, device reuse, merchant concentration, and behavioral deltas—signals that only emerge when you join multiple tables and compute features over time windows.

This project treats fraud detection as an analytics engineering problem: build staging tables, compute features, score risk, and present results in tools that analysts actually use. The product isn't just a model—it's an investigation workflow with clear risk reasons, drill-down views, and exports that document decisions for audits.

The Core Challenge

How do you turn 1.8M+ transactions into a prioritized queue of 50-100 high-signal cases per day, with explainable risk reasons that help analysts make faster, better decisions? The answer is SQL-first feature engineering + risk scoring + analyst-facing UIs.

Data Model: Why Multi-Table Architecture Matters

Real fraud signals rarely live in a single table. A transaction might look normal in isolation, but become suspicious when you see it's from a new device for an established card, or part of a velocity spike, or at a merchant with high historical risk. This project mirrors real-world fraud operations with separate entities for:

Transactions

Amount, timestamp, channel (online/in-store), merchant ID, card ID, device ID. The core fact table with ~1.8M rows.

Cards & Customers

Account age, historical spend patterns, payment history, tier (premium/standard). Used for behavioral baselines.

Devices

Device fingerprint, browser/OS, IP geolocation, reuse across accounts. Critical for detecting account takeover.

Merchants

MCC code, historical fraud rate, transaction volume, risk concentration. Helps identify risky merchant patterns.

The key insight is that fraud signals emerge from relationships between these entities. A card that's been active for 2 years suddenly making transactions from a new device is suspicious. A merchant that processes 80% of transactions from new cards is risky. A device used across 10 different accounts in 24 hours is a red flag. These patterns only become visible when you join tables and compute features over time windows.

Methodology: SQL-First Analytics Engineering

The pipeline follows a classic analytics engineering pattern: raw → staging → features → scoring → exports. Each stage is SQL-based, making it reproducible, testable, and easy to debug. Here's how it works: 

Stage 1: Raw Tables
  ├─ transactions_raw (1.8M+ rows)
  ├─ cards_raw
  ├─ devices_raw
  ├─ merchants_raw
  └─ rules_raw (alert definitions)

Stage 2: Staging (typed, cleaned, joined)
  ├─ staging.transactions (with foreign keys)
  ├─ staging.cards (with computed fields)
  ├─ staging.devices (with fingerprinting)
  └─ staging.merchants (with MCC enrichment)

Stage 3: Feature Tables (time-window aggregations)
  ├─ features.card_velocity (1h, 24h, 7d windows)
  ├─ features.device_reuse (cross-account patterns)
  ├─ features.merchant_risk (historical fraud rates)
  └─ features.behavioral_deltas (spend pattern changes)

Stage 4: Scoring (risk + explanations)
  ├─ scores.card_risk (score + top 3 reasons)
  ├─ scores.merchant_risk
  └─ scores.transaction_risk (composite)

Stage 5: BI Exports (analyst-ready views)
  ├─ bi.investigation_queue (prioritized cases)
  ├─ bi.risk_trends (time series)
  └─ bi.merchant_concentration (top risk drivers)

Feature Engineering Deep Dive

The most powerful fraud signals come from velocity patterns and behavioral deltas. Here's how I compute them:

Velocity Risk Example

WITH card_velocity AS (
  SELECT 
    card_id,
    COUNT(*) FILTER (WHERE timestamp >= NOW() - INTERVAL '1 hour') AS txn_count_1h,
    SUM(amount) FILTER (WHERE timestamp >= NOW() - INTERVAL '1 hour') AS amount_1h,
    COUNT(*) FILTER (WHERE timestamp >= NOW() - INTERVAL '24 hours') AS txn_count_24h,
    AVG(amount) OVER (PARTITION BY card_id ORDER BY timestamp 
                      ROWS BETWEEN 30 PRECEDING AND 1 PRECEDING) AS avg_amount_historical
  FROM staging.transactions
  GROUP BY card_id
)
SELECT 
  card_id,
  CASE 
    WHEN txn_count_1h > 5 THEN 'HIGH_VELOCITY'
    WHEN amount_1h > avg_amount_historical * 3 THEN 'AMOUNT_SPIKE'
    ELSE 'NORMAL'
  END AS velocity_risk_flag
FROM card_velocity

The key is that scoring is explainable: every flagged entity has "why now?" features attached. For example, a card might be flagged because:

  • 1-hour velocity spike (5+ transactions in 1 hour vs. historical average of 1-2 per hour)
  • New device for an established card (card age 2+ years, device first seen 2 hours ago)
  • Merchant risk percentile (transaction at merchant in 95th percentile for historical fraud)
  • Behavioral delta (spend pattern deviates from 30-day baseline by 2+ standard deviations)

Investigation UX: Building Tools Analysts Actually Use

The platform includes two complementary UIs: Power BI for monitoring and Streamlit for investigation. Each serves a different purpose:

Power BI: Daily Monitoring Dashboard

The Power BI dashboard provides a high-level view of fraud operations. It shows:

  • Risk trend: Time series of high-risk alerts over the past 30 days, with breakdowns by card/merchant/device
  • Merchant concentration: Which merchants and MCCs drive the most risk exposure (Pareto analysis)
  • Alert volumes: Total alerts, backlog size, average investigation time, analyst workload
  • Performance metrics: False positive rate, detection rate, time-to-detection, loss prevented

This dashboard is designed for fraud managers who need to understand operational health at a glance. It answers questions like "Are we seeing more alerts than usual?" and "Which merchants should we investigate first?"

Streamlit: Case Drill-Down Console

The Streamlit app is built for analysts investigating individual cases. When an analyst opens a flagged card, they see:

  • Card timeline: All transactions for this card, with risk flags highlighted
  • Device reuse: Which other cards have used the same device (potential account takeover)
  • Merchant history: This card's transaction history with this merchant, plus merchant's overall risk profile
  • Risk reasons: Top 3 features that drove the risk score, with values and thresholds
  • Investigator notes: Previous investigations on this card/device/merchant
  • Export: Generate a case report (PDF/Excel) with all evidence for documentation

The key is that everything is clickable and drillable. Click a transaction to see details. Click a device to see all cards that used it. Click a merchant to see its risk profile. This makes investigations faster and more thorough.

Key Insights: What the Data Revealed

After building the pipeline and running it on 1.8M+ transactions, several patterns emerged:

1. Velocity Risk is Highly Predictive

Short-window velocity spikes (1-hour and 24-hour windows) reliably surface risky cards and merchants. Cards with 5+ transactions in 1 hour had a fraud rate 8x higher than the baseline. The key is using multiple time windows—1-hour catches immediate fraud, 24-hour catches coordinated attacks, and 7-day catches gradual account takeover.

2. Merchant Concentration Drives Risk

A small subset of merchants and MCCs drive outsized risk exposure. In the dataset, the top 5% of merchants by transaction volume accounted for 40% of high-risk alerts. MCC codes like "5999" (miscellaneous retail) and "5411" (grocery stores) showed elevated fraud rates, likely due to card testing patterns. This suggests focusing investigation resources on high-risk merchants can improve efficiency.

3. Behavioral Deltas Signal Account Takeover

Deviations from historical spend patterns correlate strongly with elevated risk. Cards that suddenly switch from "low-value, frequent" to "high-value, infrequent" transactions had a fraud rate 12x higher than baseline. Similarly, cards that start transacting at new merchant categories (e.g., a card that only shopped at grocery stores suddenly making electronics purchases) showed elevated risk.

4. Device Reuse Patterns Reveal Fraud Rings

Devices used across multiple accounts in short time windows are strong fraud indicators. A device that's used by 5+ different cards within 24 hours had a fraud rate 15x higher than devices used by single cards. This pattern often indicates card testing or fraud ring activity.

Operational Impact: Targeting 25-35% Efficiency Gain

The goal isn't just to detect fraud—it's to detect it efficiently. By prioritizing cases with the highest risk scores and clearest risk reasons, analysts can focus on cases that are most likely to be fraud and easiest to investigate. The system targets a 25-35% improvement in investigation efficiency by:

  • Reducing false positives: Better feature engineering and scoring means fewer legitimate transactions flagged as suspicious. Analysts spend less time investigating false alarms.
  • Faster case resolution: Risk reasons and drill-down views help analysts understand cases faster. Instead of manually joining tables and computing features, everything is pre-computed and presented.
  • Better prioritization: Cases are ranked by risk score, so analysts tackle the highest-value cases first. This maximizes loss prevention per hour of analyst time.
  • Documentation efficiency: Case reports are auto-generated with all evidence, reducing time spent on documentation and improving audit readiness.

Technical Deep Dive: SQL Feature Engineering

The heart of the system is SQL-based feature engineering. Here are some of the most effective features:

Velocity Features

-- Card velocity over multiple time windows
WITH card_velocity AS (
  SELECT 
    card_id,
    COUNT(*) FILTER (WHERE timestamp >= NOW() - INTERVAL '1 hour') AS txn_1h,
    COUNT(*) FILTER (WHERE timestamp >= NOW() - INTERVAL '24 hours') AS txn_24h,
    COUNT(*) FILTER (WHERE timestamp >= NOW() - INTERVAL '7 days') AS txn_7d,
    SUM(amount) FILTER (WHERE timestamp >= NOW() - INTERVAL '24 hours') AS amount_24h,
    AVG(amount) OVER (PARTITION BY card_id ORDER BY timestamp 
                      ROWS BETWEEN 100 PRECEDING AND 1 PRECEDING) AS avg_amount_historical
  FROM staging.transactions
  GROUP BY card_id
)
SELECT 
  card_id,
  CASE 
    WHEN txn_1h > 5 THEN 'HIGH'
    WHEN txn_24h > 20 THEN 'MEDIUM'
    ELSE 'LOW'
  END AS velocity_risk,
  amount_24h / NULLIF(avg_amount_historical * 7, 0) AS amount_spike_ratio
FROM card_velocity

Device Reuse Features

-- Count unique cards per device in time windows
WITH device_card_counts AS (
  SELECT 
    device_id,
    COUNT(DISTINCT card_id) FILTER (WHERE timestamp >= NOW() - INTERVAL '24 hours') AS cards_24h,
    COUNT(DISTINCT card_id) FILTER (WHERE timestamp >= NOW() - INTERVAL '7 days') AS cards_7d
  FROM staging.transactions
  GROUP BY device_id
)
SELECT 
  device_id,
  CASE 
    WHEN cards_24h >= 5 THEN 'HIGH_REUSE'
    WHEN cards_7d >= 10 THEN 'MEDIUM_REUSE'
    ELSE 'LOW_REUSE'
  END AS reuse_risk_flag
FROM device_card_counts

Merchant Risk Features

Merchant risk is computed as a rolling percentile of historical fraud rates. Merchants in the 95th percentile for fraud rate get flagged, especially if they're processing high-value transactions or transactions from new cards.

Challenges & Lessons Learned

Challenge 1: Feature Engineering at Scale

Computing velocity features over 1.8M+ transactions requires efficient SQL. Window functions help, but you need to be careful about performance. I used materialized views for frequently-accessed features and indexed foreign keys for fast joins.

Challenge 2: Balancing Precision and Recall

Too many alerts overwhelm analysts; too few miss fraud. The solution is tiered scoring: high-confidence alerts go to the top of the queue, medium-confidence alerts get reviewed later, and low-confidence alerts are monitored but not prioritized. This requires calibration and feedback loops.

Challenge 3: Explainability Requirements

Analysts need to understand why a case was flagged. That's why every risk score includes the top 3 contributing features with their values. This makes investigations faster and helps analysts learn which signals matter most.

What I'd Build Next: Advanced Features

1. Feedback Loop Integration

When investigators mark cases as "fraud" or "legitimate," that feedback should recalibrate thresholds and feature weights. I'd build a weekly retraining pipeline that uses investigator dispositions to improve scoring accuracy over time.

2. Supervised Model Layer

Add a supervised model (XGBoost or LightGBM) on top of engineered features. The model would learn from historical fraud cases and provide reason codes (using SHAP values) for explainability. Monotonic constraints would ensure that higher feature values always increase risk (e.g., more velocity = more risk).

3. Cost-Based Thresholding

Optimize alert thresholds based on cost of investigation vs. loss prevented. If investigating a case costs $50 and prevents $500 in losses, it's worth it. But if investigation costs $200 and prevents $50, it's not. I'd build an optimization model that sets thresholds to maximize net benefit.

4. Production Deployment

For production, I'd add:

  • Scheduled pipelines: Run feature engineering and scoring on a schedule (hourly/daily) using Airflow or similar
  • Data quality gates: Check for missing data, schema changes, and anomalies before processing
  • Audit logs: Track all investigations, exports, and threshold changes for compliance
  • Real-time scoring: Score transactions as they arrive (not just batch), for immediate fraud detection
  • Model monitoring: Track model performance, feature drift, and alert on degradation
