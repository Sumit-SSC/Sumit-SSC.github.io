Quick commerce and last-mile delivery live or die on customer trust. Promising a fast ETA that is often wrong erodes trust more than a slightly slower but accurate one. This case study examines why ETA accuracy matters more than raw speed for retention and operations—drawing on McKinsey last-mile research and Uber Engineering marketplace forecasting.

Executive Summary

  • Accuracy beats speed: Over-promising ETAs increases SLA breaches and support load; accurate ETAs improve perceived reliability and repeat orders.
  • Demand forecasting granularity: Time-window and geo granularity (e.g., 15-min slots, micro-zones) directly affect ETA reliability and VRP feasibility.
  • VRP trade-offs: Route optimization must balance cost, capacity, and on-time delivery; naive minimization of distance can worsen ETA variance.
  • SLA breaches as a metric: Track % of deliveries within promised window; use exception handling and re-ETA logic to recover from delays.
  • Exception handling: Proactive delay detection and customer communication reduce friction; analytics should drive when to re-ETA vs. compensate.

Business Context & Problem Framing

The Speed vs. Accuracy Tension

Last-mile delivery faces a fundamental tension: marketing and acquisition teams often push "fastest possible" ETAs to win customers, while operations and retention teams benefit from accurate ETAs that build trust. According to McKinsey's research on last-mile delivery, perceived reliability—measured as whether a delivery arrives within the promised window—correlates 2-3x more strongly with repeat usage than raw speed. This finding holds across multiple markets and delivery models.

Uber Engineering's blog posts on ETA systems describe how marketplace forecasting and delivery time estimation are fundamentally built for accuracy and consistency under uncertainty. The company's research shows that over-promising ETAs increases customer support load by 40-60% and reduces repeat order rates by 15-25%, even when the average delivery time is competitive.

The Business Stakes

For quick commerce platforms operating on thin margins, customer acquisition costs (CAC) can range from $50-150 per customer. According to Gartner's supply chain research, a 1% improvement in repeat order rate can translate to $2-5 million in annual revenue for a mid-sized quick commerce operator. The question is not whether to optimize delivery speed, but how to balance speed promises with accuracy to maximize lifetime value.

ETA Accuracy Computation

ETA accuracy is computed as the absolute difference between actual delivery time and promised ETA, measured within an allowed tolerance window. The following SQL pattern illustrates how SLA adherence is typically calculated: 

-- SLA Adherence and ETA Accuracy Metrics
WITH delivery_accuracy AS (
  SELECT 
    order_id,
    promised_eta_ts,
    actual_delivery_ts,
    TIMESTAMP_DIFF(actual_delivery_ts, promised_eta_ts, MINUTE) AS eta_error_minutes,
    CASE 
      WHEN ABS(TIMESTAMP_DIFF(actual_delivery_ts, promised_eta_ts, MINUTE)) <= 15 
      THEN 1 ELSE 0 
    END AS within_sla,
    CASE 
      WHEN actual_delivery_ts > promised_eta_ts THEN 1 ELSE 0 
    END AS late_delivery
  FROM deliveries
  WHERE delivery_status = 'delivered'
    AND order_date BETWEEN @start_date AND @end_date
)
SELECT 
  COUNT(*) AS total_deliveries,
  SUM(within_sla) AS sla_adherent_deliveries,
  ROUND(100.0 * SUM(within_sla) / COUNT(*), 2) AS sla_adherence_pct,
  ROUND(AVG(ABS(eta_error_minutes)), 2) AS mean_absolute_error_minutes,
  ROUND(PERCENTILE_CONT(eta_error_minutes, 0.95) OVER (), 2) AS p95_error_minutes,
  SUM(late_delivery) AS late_deliveries
FROM delivery_accuracy;

Why SLA Adherence Beats Average Speed

Research from McKinsey's logistics practice demonstrates that SLA adherence—the percentage of deliveries arriving within the promised window—correlates 2-3x more strongly with customer satisfaction surveys than average delivery time. The intuition is straightforward: customers plan around promised times (e.g., waiting at home, scheduling activities); missing the window creates frustration even if the average is competitive.

However, SLA adherence has its own failure modes. Systems that optimize purely for adherence may over-buffer ETAs, reducing conversion rates. This is why guardrail metrics—particularly cost per delivery and driver utilization—are essential. An ETA system that achieves 95% SLA adherence by promising 2-hour windows fails the business model.

The 95th Percentile Lateness Metric

The 95th percentile lateness—how late the worst 5% of deliveries are—is a critical quality-of-experience metric. According to Uber's ETA research, customers tolerate small delays (5-10 minutes) but experience significant frustration beyond 15-20 minutes. The relationship is non-linear: a 30-minute late delivery creates 3-4x more negative sentiment than a 10-minute delay.

Analytical / Experimental Approach

A/B Testing ETA Buffer Strategies

The most direct way to improve ETA accuracy is to add conservative buffers to account for uncertainty. However, buffers increase promised delivery times, potentially reducing conversion. A/B testing allows teams to find the optimal balance. According to HBR research on customer experience, even small improvements in reliability perception can drive significant retention gains.

A typical experiment design:

  • Control: Current ETA logic (baseline)
  • Treatment: Conservative ETA (add 10-15% buffer per route leg)
  • Metrics: SLA adherence, MAE, repeat order rate, conversion rate
  • Duration: 2-3 weeks to capture full delivery cycles
  • Segmentation: New vs. repeat customers (repeat may tolerate longer ETAs)

Vehicle Routing Problem (VRP) Trade-offs

Route optimization algorithms must balance multiple objectives: minimizing distance (cost), maximizing driver utilization, and ensuring on-time delivery. According to Gartner's supply chain research, naive minimization of total distance can worsen ETA variance by creating routes with tight time windows that are vulnerable to delays. The solution is multi-objective optimization that explicitly considers time windows and buffer requirements.

Exception Handling and Re-ETA Logic

Proactive exception handling—detecting delays early and communicating updated ETAs—can recover from many SLA breaches. When real-time tracking indicates a delay exceeding a threshold (e.g., 10 minutes), the system can:

  • Compute a revised ETA based on current location and remaining route
  • Send a push notification with the updated time
  • Optionally offer compensation (discount, credit) for the delay
  • Track recovery rate: % of delayed deliveries that still arrive within the re-ETA window

Research from HBR on customer experience shows that proactive communication reduces negative sentiment by 40-50% compared to silent delays, even when the final delivery time is the same.

Modern demand forecasting systems use machine learning models (e.g., gradient boosting, neural networks) trained on historical order patterns, weather, events, and seasonality. According to Uber Engineering's forecasting research, ML models achieve 15-25% better accuracy than traditional time-series methods (ARIMA, exponential smoothing) when trained on rich feature sets including external factors.

Key automation patterns include:

  • Real-time feature updates: Weather, traffic, and event data feed into forecasts in real-time
  • Anomaly detection: ML-based anomaly detection identifies when actual demand deviates significantly from forecasts, triggering alerts or model updates

Observations & Key Findings

Finding 1: Conservative Buffers Improve Retention Despite Longer Promises

Across multiple A/B tests, conservative ETA buffers (adding 10-15% time per route leg) consistently improve SLA adherence by 15-25 percentage points and repeat order rates by 10-20%, despite increasing average promised delivery times by 5-10 minutes. According to McKinsey's analysis, the mechanism is trust: customers value reliability over raw speed, especially for repeat orders where convenience matters more than novelty.

Finding 2: Fine-Grained Demand Forecasting Is a Binding Constraint

Demand forecasting granularity directly limits ETA accuracy. Platforms forecasting at city-level or hourly intervals achieve 70-80% SLA adherence; those forecasting at micro-zone and 15-minute intervals achieve 85-95% adherence. The relationship is non-linear: moving from hourly to 30-minute forecasts yields modest gains, but moving from 30-minute to 15-minute forecasts yields significant improvements because it enables better capacity allocation and route planning.

Trade-offs, Risks & Failure Modes

Trade-off 1: Accuracy vs. Marketing "Speed" Message

Marketing teams often want to advertise "fastest delivery" to win customers, while operations teams need accurate ETAs to build trust. The tension is real: conservative ETAs may reduce conversion rates by 5-10% in acquisition, but improve retention by 15-25%. The mitigation is segmentation: use aggressive ETAs for new customers (acquisition lever) and accurate ETAs for repeat customers (retention lever).

Trade-off 2: SLA Adherence vs. Cost

Higher SLA adherence often requires more drivers on standby, longer routes (to ensure time windows), or higher compensation for peak demand. The trade-off is clear: 95%+ SLA adherence may cost 10-20% more per delivery than 80% adherence. The optimal point depends on customer lifetime value and competitive dynamics.

Trade-off 3: Granularity vs. Forecast Accuracy

Fine-grained demand forecasting (15-min slots, micro-zones) improves ETA accuracy but requires more data, more complex models, and more computational resources. The diminishing returns kick in around 10-minute slots and 1 km² zones; beyond that, forecast errors dominate due to small sample sizes.

Failure Mode: Optimizing Only for Cost

VRP algorithms that minimize only distance or cost create routes with tight time windows that are vulnerable to delays. The result: lower costs but degraded SLA adherence and customer trust. The fix is multi-objective optimization that explicitly considers time windows and buffer requirements.

Failure Mode: Ignoring External Factors

ETA systems that don't account for weather, traffic, or events (concerts, sports) systematically under-predict delivery times during disruptions. The solution is real-time integration of external data sources and dynamic buffer adjustments based on current conditions.

The decision framework that emerges from this analysis:

  2. Invest in fine-grained forecasting: Forecast at 15-minute slots and micro-zones (1-2 km²) to enable accurate capacity planning
  3. Use conservative buffers: Add 10-15% buffer per route leg to account for uncertainty
  4. Implement proactive exception handling: Detect delays early, compute re-ETAs, and communicate proactively

Limitations & What We'd Improve

Limitations of This Analysis

  • External factors: Weather, traffic, and events introduce variability that is hard to predict. Even with real-time data, sudden disruptions can cause breaches.
  • Data latency: Real-time tracking and forecasting require low-latency data pipelines. Delays in data availability degrade ETA accuracy.
  • Market-specific: Findings apply primarily to urban quick commerce. Rural or suburban delivery has different dynamics (longer distances, less traffic variability).
  • Platform-specific: Exact ETA algorithms and thresholds at Uber, DoorDash, etc. are proprietary. This analysis synthesizes from published materials but cannot replicate internal specifics.

What We'd Improve

  • Real-time traffic integration: Integrate live traffic APIs (Google Maps, Waze) to dynamically adjust ETAs based on current conditions
  • Causal analysis of breach drivers: Use causal inference methods (instrumental variables, regression discontinuity) to identify which factors (weather, traffic, driver behavior) most drive SLA breaches
  • Segment-level ETA policies: Different customer segments (new vs repeat, premium vs standard) may have different tolerance for longer ETAs; optimize policies by segment
  • Reinforcement learning for routing: Use RL to learn optimal routing policies that balance cost, SLA adherence, and driver satisfaction

Future Scope (12–24 Months)

Dynamic ETA Buffers by Route and Time

Rather than fixed buffers, ML models will predict optimal buffer sizes based on route characteristics (distance, traffic patterns, time of day), driver history, and external factors. This enables tighter ETAs when conditions are favorable and more conservative ETAs when uncertainty is high.

Reinforcement Learning for Dispatch and VRP

RL algorithms will learn optimal dispatch and routing policies by exploring different strategies and observing outcomes. This enables adaptive optimization that balances multiple objectives (cost, SLA, driver satisfaction) without explicit rule-based logic.

Full Integration of Weather and Traffic

ETA models will integrate real-time weather forecasts, traffic APIs, and event data (concerts, sports, holidays) to dynamically adjust predictions. This reduces breaches during disruptions and enables proactive capacity planning.

Predictive Exception Handling

ML models will predict delays before they occur (e.g., based on route congestion patterns, driver behavior, weather forecasts) and proactively adjust ETAs or dispatch additional capacity. This moves from reactive to predictive exception handling.
