This write-up is structured like an internal fraud analytics case study: how to go from raw payments data to an investigation queue that improves analyst efficiency and creates governance-friendly reporting (BI + exports + drill-down tooling).

Executive summary
  • Goal: turn high-volume transactions into a prioritized, explainable risk queue.
  • Approach: SQL-first staging + feature tables + risk scoring + analyst-facing UIs (Power BI monitoring + Streamlit investigation).
  • Scale: 1.8M+ transactions across cards, devices, merchants, rules, and investigator notes.

1) Business context

In payments, fraud operations live and die by triage. If every “suspicious” rule triggers an alert, analysts drown. If detection is slow, losses and regulatory exposure grow.

The product you’re building is an investigation workflow, not just a model: clear risk reasons, drill-down views, and exports that document decisions.

2) Data model (why multi-table matters)

Real fraud signals rarely live in a single table. You need device patterns, merchant history, velocity windows, and investigator feedback. This project mirrors that with separate entities for:

  • Transactions (amount, timestamp, channel)
  • Cards & customers (account age, historical behavior)
  • Devices (device fingerprint, reuse across accounts)
  • Merchants (risk concentration, MCC patterns)
  • Rules & notes (alerts, dispositions, reasons)

3) Methodology (SQL-first analytics engineering)

raw tables
  → staging (typed, cleaned, joined)
  → feature tables (velocity, merchant/device risk, behavioral deltas)
  → scoring table (risk score + explanations)
  → exports (BI-ready views)

The key is that scoring is explainable: every flagged entity should have “why now?” features attached (e.g., 1h velocity spike, new device for an established card, merchant risk percentile).

4) Investigation UX (analyst reality)

  • Power BI: daily monitoring (risk trend, merchant concentration, alert volumes, backlog).
  • Streamlit: case drill-down (card timeline, device reuse, merchant history, notes, export).
  • Exports: ensure decisions are reproducible and auditable.

5) Insights (from the project)

  • Velocity risk: short-window spikes surface risky cards/merchants effectively.
  • Merchant concentration: a small subset of merchants/MCCs can dominate exposure.
  • Behavioral deltas: deviations from historical spend patterns correlate with elevated risk.
  • Operational target: 25–35% improvement in investigation efficiency by focusing on highest-value cases.

6) What I’d build next (advanced + production)

  • Feedback loop: dispositions from investigators → recalibrate thresholds/features weekly.
  • Model layer: add supervised model on top of engineered features with reason codes (SHAP / monotonic constraints).
  • Alert tuning: cost-based thresholding to optimize loss prevented vs analyst time.
  • Deployment: scheduled pipelines, data quality gates, and audit logs for investigations.
