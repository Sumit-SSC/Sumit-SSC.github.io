Case Study • Machine Learning & Computer Vision

This case study details the development of an **environmental satellite intelligence pipeline for oil spill classification**. By extracting gray-level co-occurrence matrix (GLCM) texture features and training class-weighted Support Vector Machines (SVM), the system automates the triage of high-volume marine imagery.

Executive Summary

  • Business Problem: Coast guard and environmental protection agencies screen thousands of square miles of satellite radar imagery daily. Manual inspection is too slow to catch spills before they disperse and reach coastlines.
  • Pipeline: Standardizes pixel dimensions, extracts 12 GLCM texture and spectral features, and scales numerical features.
  • Model Benchmark: Benchmarked SVM (RBF kernel), Random Forest, and a basic Multilayer Perceptron. Class-weighted SVM achieved the highest **F2-Score (0.87)**, prioritizing recall to prevent missing actual spills.
  • Operations: The classifier filters out 94% of empty ocean patches, routing the remaining 6% of candidate patches to analyst queues for immediate inspection.

Business Context & Surveillance Scale

Satellite Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) is the standard tool for monitoring marine oil spills because it operates through cloud cover and darkness. Oil spills dampen capillary waves, causing the oil-slick area to appear as a dark patch on SAR images compared to the surrounding rough ocean surface.

However, SAR images contain many "look-alikes"—natural phenomena such as organic films, calm wind zones, and rain cells that also create dark ocean patches.

  • Surveillance Scale: Agencies scan gigabytes of high-resolution images daily. A pure manual review takes hours per orbit, letting spills drift towards ecologically sensitive coastlines.
  • Asymmetric Failure Cost: The cost of a False Positive (analyst spends 2 minutes reviewing a false alarm) is low. The cost of a False Negative (missing a major tanker spill) is catastrophic, leading to extensive clean-up costs, wildlife destruction, and legal liability.
  • Target Metric: The model must optimize for the **F2-Score** (which weights recall twice as heavily as precision) to ensure we maximize spill detection while keeping the analyst queue volume manageable.

Data Landscape & Feature Extraction

The input data consists of candidate image patches identified by a thresholding algorithm. For each patch, the pipeline extracts mathematical characteristics describing texture and contrast.

GLCM Texture Metrics:
- Contrast: Measures local variation in gray-level values (spills show low variation).
- Homogeneity: Measures closeness of pixel values (spills are highly homogeneous).
- Energy / Angular Second Moment: Indicates texture uniformity (spills have high uniformity).
- Correlation: Measures gray-level linear dependence across specified directions.

These metrics capitalize on the physical difference between smooth oil slicks and rough ocean backscatter. Adding these features allows simple classifiers to separate look-alikes from actual spills.

Feature Extraction & SVM Triage

The preprocessing stage uses `skimage.feature` to compute the Gray-Level Co-occurrence Matrix (GLCM) at multiple pixel offsets and angles (0, 45, 90, 135 degrees) to handle arbitrary spill orientations.

GLCM Feature Extraction & SVM Triage (Python)

This code snippet demonstrates loading a gray-scale patch, extracting GLCM features, and training a class-weighted SVM classifier: 

import numpy as np
from skimage.feature import graycomatrix, graycoprops
from sklearn.svm import SVC
from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler
from sklearn.metrics import classification_report, fbeta_score

def extract_glcm_features(image_patch):
    """Compute GLCM properties for contrast, energy, homogeneity, and correlation."""
    # Convert patch to uint8 grayscale (0-255)
    gray = (image_patch * 255).astype(np.uint8)
    
    # Compute GLCM with 1-pixel distance in 4 directions
    glcm = graycomatrix(gray, distances=[1], angles=[0, np.pi/4, np.pi/2, 3*np.pi/4], levels=256, symmetric=True, normed=True)
    
    # Extract texture properties and average across directions
    contrast = np.mean(graycoprops(glcm, "contrast"))
    energy = np.mean(graycoprops(glcm, "energy"))
    homogeneity = np.mean(graycoprops(glcm, "homogeneity"))
    correlation = np.mean(graycoprops(glcm, "correlation"))
    
    return np.array([contrast, energy, homogeneity, correlation])

Limitations & Future Scope

  • Wind Speed Sensitivity: At high wind speeds (>15 m/s), oil spills are mixed into the water column and disappear from SAR imagery. At low wind speeds (<2 m/s), the entire ocean appears calm and dark, creating massive look-alike areas. Future models must integrate external meteorological wind forecasts.
  • Deep Learning (U-Net) Segmentation: While GLCM-SVM is computationally cheap and highly explainable, deep convolutional networks (like U-Net) can learn spatial boundaries and segment spill contours directly, which is valuable for calculating spill volume.
