This case study documents an environmental ML pipeline for classifying oil-spill vs non-spill satellite patches. The key goal is a workflow that is reproducible and interpretable enough for screening and human review—not a black-box demo.

Executive Summary

  • Approach: preprocessing + engineered texture/spectral features + model comparison (RF, SVM, NN baseline).
  • Evaluation: confusion matrix + ROC + precision/recall to balance false alarms vs missed spills.
  • Deliverable: a documented notebook so reviewers can reproduce results and understand failure modes.

Problem

Satellite imagery screening is high volume. Manual review is slow and inconsistent. An ML classifier can triage patches so analysts focus on the highest-probability spill candidates and respond faster.

Methodology

  1. Preprocessing. Normalize inputs; standardize train/validation split; optional augmentation strategy.
  2. Feature extraction. Texture descriptors and other engineered signals that capture spill-like patterns.
  3. Modeling. Compare Random Forest and SVM (interpretability + performance) with a neural baseline.
  4. Evaluation. Report per-class metrics; analyze false positives/false negatives as operational cost trade-offs.

Operational framing

  • False positives: waste analyst time but may be acceptable if downstream review is cheap.
  • False negatives: missed spills are expensive and reputationally risky—often the metric to protect.
  • Thresholding: choose decision thresholds based on capacity and risk tolerance (not just accuracy).

Next Steps

  • Calibration: probability calibration to make scores usable for triage queues.
  • Monitoring: drift detection as satellite sources and conditions change.
  • Explainability: feature importance summaries to validate model focus and reduce brittle behavior.
