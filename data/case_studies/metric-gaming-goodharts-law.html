When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure (Goodhart's Law). This case study examines metric gaming, common gaming patterns, and how to design guardrails—using HBR and Gartner KPI Design.

Executive Summary

  • Goodhart's Law: Once a metric is tied to incentives or targets, people optimize for the metric rather than the underlying outcome; the metric's validity degrades.
  • Gaming patterns: Selection (who or what counts), definition creep (expanding the numerator/denominator), effort substitution (doing what moves the number, not what creates value).
  • Guardrails: Lock definitions; use multiple metrics (primary + guardrails); audit and segment to detect gaming; avoid incentive design that rewards one number in isolation.

Business Context & Problem Framing

Goodhart's Law: When a Measure Becomes a Target

Economist Charles Goodhart observed: "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." Once a metric is tied to incentives, bonuses, or performance reviews, people optimize for the metric rather than the underlying outcome. According to Harvard Business Review's "When Metrics Mislead", this is one of the most pervasive failure modes in data-driven organizations—metrics that were designed to proxy for outcomes become decoupled from those outcomes when behavior adapts to the metric. The solution is not to abandon metrics but to design them with gaming in mind: locked definitions, guardrails, and audits.

Gartner's KPI design research emphasizes that unchecked target-setting leads to selection (who or what counts), definition creep (expanding the numerator or denominator), and effort substitution (doing what moves the number rather than what creates value). Organizations that adopt Goodhart-aware design—locked definitions, multiple metrics (primary + guardrails), segment audits, and incentive conditioning—see metrics remain valid under pressure and fewer unintended distortions.

The Business Stakes

When metrics are gamed, organizations see vanity gains—the number goes up while true outcomes (revenue, retention, quality) stagnate or decline. According to Reforge's North Star framework, companies that optimize for engagement metrics without guardrails often see engagement rise while customer lifetime value and retention fall. The fix is to lock definitions, use guardrails that must not regress, audit by segment to detect gaming, and condition incentives on multiple outcomes so that no single number can be gamed in isolation.

Data Landscape & Event Model

Auditable Metrics: Definitions and Lineage

To detect and prevent gaming, metrics must be auditable: clear definitions (who counts, what counts), source events, and calculation logic. According to Gartner's KPI design research, organizations should store definition version and calculation logic in a metric catalog or semantic layer (e.g., dbt metrics, Looker dimensions); when definitions change, the change should be versioned and historical series recalculated or annotated so that trends are comparable. Segment by channel, cohort, and geography so that selection or definition creep can be detected (e.g., "primary metric up 20% but only in one channel with looser definition"). 

-- Example: Metric definition and segment audit
-- Primary: WAU (weekly active users), definition v2.1
-- WAU = distinct user_id where event_type = 'session_start' in last 7 days
WITH weekly_active AS (
  SELECT date_trunc('week', event_date) AS week,
         user_id, channel, cohort_week
  FROM events
  WHERE event_type = 'session_start'
    AND event_date >= CURRENT_DATE - 90
  GROUP BY week, user_id, channel, cohort_week
)
SELECT week, channel, cohort_week,
       COUNT(DISTINCT user_id) AS wau,
       COUNT(DISTINCT user_id) * 1.0 / SUM(COUNT(DISTINCT user_id)) OVER (PARTITION BY week) AS pct_of_total
FROM weekly_active
GROUP BY week, channel, cohort_week;
-- Audit: flag when pct_of_total for one channel spikes (selection)

Guardrail Metrics and Independent Data

Guardrail metrics—metrics that must not regress (e.g., NPS, quality, compliance)—should come from the same or independent data with locked definitions. According to Reforge, when guardrails are from independent sources (e.g., survey for NPS, support system for tickets), they are harder to game than metrics derived from the same event stream as the primary. Store guardrail definitions and segment breakdowns so that gaming (e.g., primary up, guardrail down) is visible.

Metrics, KPIs & North Star

Primary Metric with Locked Definition

The primary metric (North Star) should have a locked definition: who counts (e.g., "user with at least one session"), what counts (e.g., "session = app open with >5 seconds"), and calculation logic (e.g., COUNT DISTINCT user_id per day). According to Gartner's KPI design research, locking the definition and versioning changes reduces definition creep—the primary driver of metric decay when incentives attach.

Primary (North Star)

  • One metric, locked definition
  • Versioned, auditable
  • Segment breakdown for audit

Guardrails

  • Must not regress (e.g., NPS, retention)
  • Condition incentives on guardrails
  • Independent definition where possible

Design Rule: Condition Incentives on Guardrails

Do not incentivize the primary metric in isolation. According to Reforge's North Star framework, make incentives conditional on guardrails: e.g., bonus only if primary improves and guardrails (NPS, retention, compliance) do not regress. This reduces effort substitution and selection—teams cannot game the primary at the expense of guardrails without losing the incentive.

Analytical / Experimental Approach

Segment Primary Metric to Detect Selection

Segment the primary metric by cohort, channel, and geography; flag when one segment improves sharply while others stagnate or decline—a sign of selection (e.g., acquiring easy, low-value users to boost DAU). According to Gartner's KPI design research, organizations that run quarterly segment-level audits of the primary metric detect selection 2–3x faster than those that only track the aggregate.

Monitor Correlation Between Primary and Guardrails

When the primary metric and guardrails are correlated (e.g., DAU and retention), gaming the primary often shows up as guardrail decay—retention falls while DAU rises. Monitor the correlation over time; when correlation decays (primary up, guardrails flat or down), it suggests gaming. According to Reforge, this is a leading indicator of Goodhart failure; trigger review and corrective action.

Pre-Register Definitions and Periodic Review

Pre-register metric definitions in a catalog; require approval for definition changes. Conduct periodic (e.g., annual) review of metrics with leadership: is the primary still the right proxy? Are guardrails still binding? Have definitions crept? According to HBR, culture and process are as important as design—metrics that are reviewed and audited stay valid longer.

AI & Automation Layer

Automated Segment-Level Reporting

Automated dashboards that surface the primary metric by segment (channel, cohort, geography) enable continuous audit. According to Gartner, when guardrails and primary are shown together with segment breakdowns, teams are less likely to game—and anomalies (e.g., one channel spiking while others flat) are visible immediately.

Alerts and Anomaly Detection

Alerts when guardrails breach (e.g., NPS drops below threshold) or when segment mix shifts sharply (e.g., one channel's share of primary metric jumps 20% week-over-week) ensure that gaming is caught early. Optional ML-based anomaly detection on segment-level metrics can flag when one segment is gaming (e.g., primary up, guardrails down for that segment). According to HBR's GenAI research, automation of routine audit tasks frees analysts for deeper investigation.

Definition Registry and Change Log

A definition registry (e.g., dbt metrics, Looker dimensions, or dedicated catalog) stores current definitions and calculation logic; a change log records every definition change with timestamp, owner, and rationale. This supports auditability and slows definition creep—changes require approval and are visible to all.

Observations & Key Findings

Finding 1: Gaming Is Predictable When One Metric Is Heavily Incentivized

Across industries and company sizes, when a single metric is tied to bonuses or targets without guardrails, gaming appears within 6–18 months. According to HBR's "When Metrics Mislead", the pattern is selection, definition creep, or effort substitution—or a combination. The fix is to design metrics with gaming in mind from the start: locked definitions, guardrails, and segment audits.

Finding 2: Locked Definitions and Multi-Metric Frameworks Reduce Gaming

Organizations that adopt locked definitions (versioned, change log) and multi-metric frameworks (primary + guardrails, incentives conditional on guardrails) see 40–60% fewer gaming-related incidents (e.g., guardrail breaches, segment anomalies). According to Gartner, the key is making guardrails binding—not just visible—so that teams cannot optimize the primary at the expense of other outcomes without losing the incentive.

Finding 3: Audits and Segment Transparency Are Necessary

Locked definitions and guardrails reduce gaming but do not eliminate it—people find new ways to move the number. According to Reforge, audits (quarterly segment-level review of primary and guardrails) and segment transparency (dashboards that show primary by channel, cohort) are necessary to detect and correct gaming early. Culture matters: when leadership rewards outcome over number and questions metric validity, gaming is less likely to persist.

Trade-offs, Risks & Failure Modes

Trade-off: Simplicity vs. Robustness

One number (primary only) is simple to communicate and incentivize but invites gaming. Definitions + guardrails + audits add complexity but keep the metric valid under pressure. According to Gartner, the rule of thumb is to add guardrails and lock definitions when the primary is tied to incentives and the team has levers that could move it in harmful ways.

Risk: Guardrails Get Gamed Too

If guardrails are narrow (e.g., only NPS), teams may optimize for NPS in ways that harm other outcomes (e.g., survey manipulation). Use a broad set of guardrails (quality, compliance, long-term outcome) and qualitative review (e.g., spot-checks) to reduce guardrail gaming.

Failure Mode: Changing the Metric Without Fixing Incentives

When the primary metric "breaks" (proxy decay, gaming), some organizations simply switch to a new metric without changing incentive design. The new metric is then gamed in turn. The fix is to adopt Goodhart-aware design (definitions, guardrails, conditioning) when introducing the new metric, not to repeat the single-metric mistake.

Business Impact & Decision Outcomes

Quantified Impact

Based on published case studies and industry benchmarks:

  • Metric validity: Organizations that adopt Goodhart-aware design (locked definitions, guardrails, segment audits) see metrics remain valid 2–3x longer before proxy decay or gaming forces a change (Gartner).
  • Fewer unintended distortions: Incentive conditioning (bonus only if guardrails OK) reduces effort substitution and selection; teams report fewer "vanity metric" wins and more focus on true outcomes.
  • Faster detection: Segment-level audits and alerts detect gaming 2–3x faster than aggregate-only monitoring, enabling corrective action before the metric breaks.

Decision Framework Applied

  1. Adopt Goodhart-aware KPI design: locked definitions, guardrails, segment audits, incentive conditioning.
  2. Do not incentivize the primary without conditioning on guardrails.
  3. Maintain definition registry and change log; require approval for definition changes.
  4. Run quarterly segment-level audits; alert when guardrails breach or segment mix shifts sharply.
  5. Conduct annual metric review with leadership; reassess primary and guardrails when strategy or product changes.

Limitations & What We'd Improve

Limitations

  • Guardrails can be gamed: If guardrails are narrow or poorly defined, teams may optimize for them in harmful ways. Use broad guardrails and qualitative review.
  • Culture and incentives are hard to change: Moving from single-metric to multi-metric incentives requires leadership buy-in and communication; resistance is common.
  • Definition lock-in can slow adaptation: When the business or product changes, definitions may need to change; balance consistency with flexibility.

What We'd Improve

  • Outcome-based metrics: LTV, customer health score, and other outcome-based metrics are harder to game than leading indicators; use them as primary or guardrails where feasible.
  • Randomized audits: Randomly select segments or time periods for deep audit to keep teams honest without auditing everything.
  • Incentive experiments: A/B test incentive schemes (primary only vs. primary + guardrails) to measure impact on gaming and true outcomes.
