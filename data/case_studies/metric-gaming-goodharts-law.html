Why It Matters: Explains how metrics distort behavior and how to design guardrails.

When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure (Goodhart's Law). This case study examines metric gaming, common gaming patterns, and how to design guardrails—using HBR and Gartner KPI Design.

Executive Summary

  • Goodhart's Law: Once a metric is tied to incentives or targets, people optimize for the metric rather than the underlying outcome; the metric's validity degrades.
  • Gaming patterns: Selection (who or what counts), definition creep (expanding the numerator/denominator), effort substitution (doing what moves the number, not what creates value).
  • Guardrails: Lock definitions; use multiple metrics (primary + guardrails); audit and segment to detect gaming; avoid incentive design that rewards one number in isolation.

Business Context & Problem Framing

Organizations set KPIs to align behavior. HBR's "When Metrics Mislead" and Gartner's KPI design work describe how unchecked target-setting leads to gaming and unintended consequences. The solution is not to abandon metrics but to design them with gaming in mind: definitions, guardrails, and audits.

Data Landscape & Event Model

Metrics must be auditable: clear definitions, source events, and segment breakdowns. Store definition version and calculation logic; segment by channel, cohort, and geography to detect selection or creep. Guardrail metrics should come from the same or independent data with locked definitions.

Metrics, KPIs & North Star

Primary metric: one North Star with a locked definition. Guardrails: metrics that must not regress (e.g., quality, compliance, long-term outcome). Design rule: do not incentivize the primary without conditioning on guardrails; audit primary by segment to detect gaming.

Analytical / Experimental Approach

Segment primary metric by cohort, channel, and time; flag when a segment improves at the expense of others (selection). Monitor correlation between primary and guardrails over time (decay suggests gaming). Pre-register definitions and review them periodically.

AI & Automation Layer

Automated segment-level reporting; alerts when guardrails breach or when segment mix shifts sharply; definition registry and change log; optional anomaly detection on segment-level metrics.

Observations & Key Findings

Gaming is predictable when one metric is heavily incentivized; locked definitions and multi-metric frameworks reduce but do not eliminate gaming; audits and segment transparency are necessary to detect and correct.

Trade-offs, Risks & Failure Modes

Trade-off: simplicity (one number) vs. robustness (definitions + guardrails). Risk: guardrails themselves get gamed. Failure mode: changing the metric when it "breaks" without fixing incentive design.

Business Impact & Decision Outcomes

Decision: adopt Goodhart-aware KPI design—locked definitions, guardrails, segment audits, and incentive conditioning. Outcome: metrics that remain valid under pressure and fewer unintended distortions.

Limitations & What We'd Improve

Guardrails can be gamed; culture and incentives are hard to change. Improvements: outcome-based metrics (e.g., LTV) that are harder to game; randomized audits; incentive experiments.

Future Scope (12–24 Months)

Automated gaming detection; causal metrics (effect of action on outcome); regulatory and ESG alignment of KPIs.

Key Takeaway

Goodhart's Law is inevitable when a single metric is targeted. Mitigate by locking definitions, using guardrails, auditing by segment, and conditioning incentives on multiple outcomes so that metrics remain useful under pressure.
