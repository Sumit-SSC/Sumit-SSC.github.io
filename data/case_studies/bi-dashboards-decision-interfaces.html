BI dashboards should be decision interfaces—designed so that the right person sees the right metric at the right time and can act. This case study covers KPI layering, narrative dashboards, and exec vs ops views—using Gartner and Tableau dashboard design.

Executive Summary

  • KPI layering: Primary metric first; secondary and guardrails in context; avoid metric overload—one decision per view.
  • Narrative dashboards: Lead with the question or decision; use layout and order to tell a story (what happened, why, what to do).
  • Exec vs ops views: Exec: high-level, trend, exception; ops: granular, filterable, actionable. Same data, different slice and depth.

Business Context & Problem Framing

Why Dashboards Fail Without a Decision Lens

BI dashboards should be decision interfaces—designed so that the right person sees the right metric at the right time and can act. According to Gartner's BI best practices, dashboards fail when they show data without guiding the viewer to a decision: "data dumps" with dozens of charts lead to low engagement and no clear action. Tableau's dashboard design guidance emphasizes designing for the user (exec vs ops), the question (what happened vs what to do), and the action (monitor vs intervene). When dashboards lead with a single primary metric and a clear narrative, engagement and follow-through improve.

Exec vs Ops: Same Data, Different Depth

Exec views need high-level trends, one North Star KPI, and exception lists—"what's wrong and where?" Ops views need granular filters, drill-down tables, and actionable detail—"who do I call, what do I do?" According to HBR's data-driven decision research, the same underlying data can serve both when the interface is tailored: exec sees summary and exceptions; ops sees the same data with filters and drill-down. Reforge and Amplitude's dashboard design recommend one primary KPI per section and narrative order (what happened → why → what to do) so that the dashboard tells a story rather than dumping metrics.

Data Landscape & Event Model

Curated Datasets and Semantic Layer

Dashboards consume curated datasets (aggregated, filtered) with clear definitions. According to dbt Labs' analytics engineering guidance, a semantic layer or metric definitions (e.g., "active user" = definition + dimensions) ensure consistency across exec and ops views—both pull from the same logic. Exec and ops views may use the same base tables with different aggregation and filters (e.g., exec: company-wide trend; ops: region × product drill-down). Gartner recommends exposing data freshness and definition version on the dashboard so that viewers know when data was last updated and what "active user" means.

Metrics, KPIs & North Star

Primary KPI and Guardrails

Primary KPI per dashboard or section—one number that answers "how are we doing?" Secondary metrics in context (e.g., trend, breakdown). Guardrails (e.g., data freshness, definition version) visible so that viewers know when to trust the number. According to Reforge's North Star framework, dashboards that lead with one primary metric get 2–3x more engagement than those that show many metrics equally. North star for the dashboard itself: it drives at least one clear decision per user type—e.g., "should I escalate?" or "which accounts to call?"

Analytical / Experimental Approach

User Research and Metric Mapping

User research: What decision does the viewer need? Map metrics to decisions—e.g., "revenue down?" → show revenue trend, segment breakdown, top decliners. Design layout in narrative order (what happened → why → what to do) and hierarchy (primary KPI largest, secondary in context). According to Tableau, dashboards designed with a clear "so what?" get more clicks and follow-up actions.

A/B Test Layout and Defaults

A/B test layout (e.g., narrative order vs. metric grid) or default filters (e.g., time range, segment) to measure engagement (views, time on dashboard) and action (clicks, exports, follow-up). According to HBR's analytics research, organizations that test dashboard design see 20–30% improvement in "dashboard led to an action" when they lead with one question and one primary metric.

AI & Automation Layer

Narrative Summaries and Anomaly Highlights

Auto-generated narrative summaries (LLM) from dashboard data—e.g., "Revenue was $X, up Y% vs last week; top driver: segment Z"—reduce time to interpret. Anomaly highlights (e.g., segments that moved most) surface "why" without manual drill-down. According to HBR's GenAI research, natural-language summaries and optional NL questions that route to the right dashboard or slice will close the gap between "I have a question" and "I see the answer."

Observations & Key Findings

Finding 1: One Primary Metric and a Question Drive Action

Dashboards that lead with a question (e.g., "Is revenue on track?") and one primary metric get more action than those that show many metrics equally. According to Gartner and Tableau, the "one primary per section" rule reduces cognitive load and focuses the viewer on the decision.

Finding 2: Exec and Ops Need Different Depth

Exec and ops need different depth—exec: trend and exceptions; ops: filters and drill-down. Same underlying data with different slice and layout serves both; building one "universal" dashboard often satisfies no one. According to Amplitude, role-based views (exec vs ops) improve both engagement and action.

Finding 3: Narrative Order and Exception Highlighting Improve Usefulness

Narrative order (what happened → why → what to do) and exception highlighting (e.g., "these 5 segments are down") improve usefulness—viewers know where to look and what to do. Reforge and industry benchmarks report 20–30% higher "led to action" when dashboards include a clear "what to do" panel (e.g., exception list with link to drill-down).

Trade-offs, Risks & Failure Modes

Trade-off: Simplicity vs Completeness

Few metrics (simplicity) focus action but may miss context; many metrics (completeness) provide context but overwhelm. According to Gartner, the rule of thumb is one primary per section with secondary and drill-down available—not everything on one screen.

Risk and Failure Mode

Risk: Dashboard becomes a data dump when stakeholders ask for "just one more metric" and the primary is lost. Failure mode: Building for "everyone" and satisfying no one—exec finds it too detailed, ops finds it too high-level. Design for specific personas (exec, ops) and map to their decisions.

Business Impact & Decision Outcomes

Quantified Impact

Based on industry benchmarks: dashboards designed as decision interfaces (KPI layering, narrative, exec vs ops) see 20–30% higher engagement and "led to action" (Gartner, Tableau). Outcome: higher engagement and clearer action from BI when design is user- and decision-centric.

Decision Framework Applied

  1. Design dashboards as decision interfaces—one primary KPI per section, narrative order, exec vs ops views.
  2. Map metrics to decisions; expose freshness and definition so viewers know when to trust.
  3. A/B test layout and defaults; measure engagement and action.

Limitations & What We'd Improve

Static dashboards can lag behind new questions; personalization is limited. Improvements: Adaptive defaults (e.g., time range by role); NL-to-dashboard (question → right dashboard or slice); tighter link to alerts and workflows so that "what to do" triggers an action (e.g., create ticket, notify owner).

Future Scope (12–24 Months)

Conversational BI: Natural language questions that drive to the right dashboard or slice. Dashboards that suggest next question or action: "Revenue is down—view segment breakdown?" or "Create follow-up task." Integration with decision automation: Exception panel that triggers workflow (e.g., alert, task, CRM update). HBR's GenAI research points to BI that closes the loop from insight to action.
