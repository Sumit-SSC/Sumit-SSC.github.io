Type: Decision System Architecture

Suggested Source: Gartner — Decision Intelligence

What it Shows: Dashboard → alert → action → feedback. Decision automation, alerting, ownership.

Why It Matters: Close the analytics-to-action gap.

Dashboards inform; decision systems close the loop from insight to action. This case study examines how to move from dashboards to decision systems: decision automation, alerting systems, and ownership models—using Gartner Decision Intelligence and McKinsey analytics to action.

Executive Summary

  • Decision automation: When a metric or rule triggers (e.g., threshold breach), automatically trigger an action (e.g., alert, workflow, API call) so that insight leads to action without manual check.
  • Alerting systems: Define thresholds and owners; alert when breached; track acknowledgment and resolution. Reduce "dashboard graveyard" by pushing only actionable alerts.
  • Ownership models: Who owns the metric, the alert, and the action? Define RACI (responsible, accountable, consulted, informed) so that decision systems have clear owners and escalation paths.

Business Context & Problem Framing

Gartner's Decision Intelligence and McKinsey's analytics-to-action describe how most analytics value is lost between insight and action. Dashboards alone do not drive decisions unless someone looks, interprets, and acts. Decision systems close the gap: alerts, automation, and ownership so that the right person takes the right action at the right time.

Type: Flow — Dashboard to Action

Suggested Source: McKinsey — Analytics to Action

What it Shows: Metric → threshold → alert → owner → action (e.g., workflow, ticket) → feedback (resolution).

Why It Matters: Close the loop from insight to action.

Data Landscape & Event Model

Metrics: metric_id, value, timestamp, dimensions. Alerts: alert_id, metric_id, threshold, condition, owner, triggered_at, acknowledged_at, resolved_at. Actions: action_id, alert_id, action_type (e.g., workflow, ticket), outcome. Ownership: metric_id, owner, escalation_path. Use to monitor alert coverage, response time, and resolution.

Metrics, KPIs & North Star

Primary: % critical metrics with alerts and owners; mean time to acknowledge and resolve. Secondary: alert volume, false positive rate, action rate (e.g., % alerts that lead to action). North star: every critical metric has an owner and an alert; insights lead to action within SLA.

Analytical / Experimental Approach

Audit: which metrics have alerts and owners? Add alerts and owners for critical metrics; define thresholds and escalation. A/B test alert design (e.g., threshold, frequency) on response rate and action rate. Monitor alert fatigue (e.g., volume, acknowledgment rate) and tune thresholds.

AI & Automation Layer

Automated threshold evaluation and alerting; workflow triggers (e.g., create ticket, notify owner); optional anomaly-based alerts (ML); LLM summarization of "what happened and what to do" for alert context; ownership and escalation in tooling.

Observations & Key Findings

Alerts and ownership dramatically increase action rate vs. dashboards alone; decision automation (e.g., auto-trigger workflow) closes the loop further; alert fatigue is reduced when thresholds are tuned and only actionable alerts are sent.

Trade-offs, Risks & Failure Modes

Trade-off: alert volume (sensitive) vs. missed events (insensitive). Risk: alert fatigue; over-automation. Failure mode: dashboards without alerts or owners—no one acts.

Business Impact & Decision Outcomes

Decision: move from dashboards to decision systems—alerts, automation, ownership. Outcome: analytics that drives action and measurable impact.

Limitations & What We'd Improve

Not all metrics are actionable; ownership can be ambiguous. Improvements: decision catalog (which metrics drive which decisions); integration with workflow and ticketing; feedback loop (did action resolve the issue?).

Future Scope (12–24 Months)

Full decision automation (auto-action within guardrails); causal alerts (why did metric change?); integration with operational runbooks.
