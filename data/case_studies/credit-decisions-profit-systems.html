Why It Matters: Frames credit as profit optimization, not just classification.

Credit approval is often treated as a binary approve/decline problem. In practice, it is a profit optimization problem: expected profit = revenue from good borrowers minus loss from bad, adjusted for exposure and loss given default. This case study examines PD × LGD × EAD frameworks, reject inference, vintage analysis, and bias and governance—using McKinsey credit risk research and the Basel framework.

Executive Summary

  • Profit, not accuracy: Optimize for expected profit (revenue − expected loss) per applicant; classification accuracy is a means, not the end.
  • PD × LGD × EAD: Probability of default, loss given default, and exposure at default decompose risk and revenue; scorecards and models should feed these components.
  • Reject inference: Applicants who were declined have no outcome; use augmentation (e.g., parceling, reweighting) or accept selection bias and monitor portfolio performance.
  • Vintage analysis: Cohort performance over time (e.g., default rate by origination month) reveals model drift, policy impact, and economic cycles.
  • Bias & governance: Fairness metrics (e.g., adverse action, disparity in approval/default by protected group) and model documentation for regulatory and internal governance.

Business Context & Problem Framing

Lenders maximize long-term profit by approving applicants whose expected revenue exceeds expected loss. McKinsey's credit risk analytics work emphasizes moving from "good/bad" scoring to economic decision frameworks. Basel capital and IFRS 9 ECL rely on PD, LGD, and EAD; analytics teams must produce estimates that are consistent with both profit and regulation.

Data Landscape & Event Model

Application (applicant_id, features, decision, score); performance (account_id, monthly_snapshot, default_flag, EAD, recovery); reject pool (applicant_id, features, no outcome). PD models use performance data; reject inference augments with rejected applicants via parceling or reweighting. Vintage = cohort by origination date; track default rate and loss by vintage and month.

Metrics, KPIs & North Star

Primary: Expected profit per decision (or portfolio NIM / loss ratio). Secondary: PD/LGD calibration, approval rate, default rate by segment. Guardrails: Fairness metrics, capital adequacy, model stability. North star: risk-adjusted profit within governance constraints.

Analytical / Experimental Approach

Backtest profit at different score cutoffs; A/B test policy (e.g., new cutoff or new model) on approval rate, default rate, and profit. Vintage analysis: default rate by origination cohort and age; monitor drift. Reject inference: compare portfolio performance with and without augmentation; document assumptions.

AI & Automation Layer

Automated PD/LGD model retraining with vintage and reject-inference; profit-curve dashboards; fairness dashboards (approval/default by segment); model documentation and change logs for governance.

Observations & Key Findings

Profit-optimal cutoff often differs from accuracy-optimal; vintage analysis surfaces policy and economic effects; reject inference improves representativeness but adds assumption risk; fairness constraints can be incorporated as constraints on threshold or segment-level policies.

Trade-offs, Risks & Failure Modes

Trade-off: profit vs. volume vs. fairness. Risk: reject inference mis-specification; model drift in downturns. Failure mode: optimizing only for default rate can shrink volume and profit; optimizing only for volume can blow loss.

Business Impact & Decision Outcomes

Decision: adopt profit-based decision framework; use PD × LGD × EAD; run vintage analysis and reject inference with clear assumptions; enforce fairness and governance. Outcome: better risk-adjusted returns and auditability.

Limitations & What We'd Improve

Reject inference is unobservable; PD/LGD in low default portfolios. Improvements: alternative reject inference methods; stress testing; segment-level profit curves.

Future Scope (12–24 Months)

Causal credit policies; explainable adverse action; integration with IFRS 9 and Basel reporting.

Key Takeaway

Treat credit decisions as profit systems: optimize for expected profit using PD × LGD × EAD, support with reject inference and vintage analysis, and embed bias and governance checks so that risk-adjusted returns and fairness are both delivered.
