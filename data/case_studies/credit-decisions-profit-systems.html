Business Context & Problem Framing

From Classification to Profit Optimization

Credit approval is often treated as a binary approve/decline problem: score applicants, set a cutoff, approve above and decline below. In practice, lending is a profit optimization problem. According to McKinsey's credit risk analytics research, lenders maximize long-term profit by approving applicants whose expected revenue (interest, fees) exceeds expected loss (default × loss given default). A high-risk applicant with high revenue potential may be more profitable than a low-risk applicant with thin margins.

The Basel framework and IFRS 9 (Expected Credit Loss) rely on three core components: Probability of Default (PD), Loss Given Default (LGD), and Exposure at Default (EAD). Analytics teams must produce estimates that are consistent with both profit optimization and regulatory capital and provisioning. This dual objective—profit and compliance—drives the need for transparent, auditable models and decision frameworks.

The Business Stakes

For retail lenders, a 1% improvement in risk-adjusted profit per approved account can translate to tens of millions in annual profit. According to Gartner's credit risk research, lenders that shift from accuracy-optimized scorecards to profit-optimized decision frameworks see 10-20% improvements in portfolio economics. The mechanism: profit-optimal cutoffs often differ from accuracy-optimal cutoffs; approving more marginal but profitable applicants increases volume and revenue without proportionally increasing loss.

However, profit optimization must be balanced with fairness and governance. ECOA (Equal Credit Opportunity Act) and fair lending regulations require that credit decisions do not discriminate on protected characteristics. Analytics teams must design profit frameworks that incorporate fairness constraints—either as hard constraints (e.g., no disparate impact) or as guardrail metrics monitored alongside profit.

Data Landscape & Event Model

Application and Decision Data

Credit analytics requires tracking the full lifecycle: application, decision, account performance, and default/recovery. The typical schema captures application-level features (bureau score, income, debt-to-income, employment), decision (approve/decline, limit, pricing), and outcome (default flag, EAD at default, recovery amount). According to McKinsey's credit risk research, data quality and consistency across these stages are critical for PD/LGD estimation and profit calibration.

Reject Inference: The Missing-Outcome Problem

A fundamental challenge in credit analytics is reject inference: applicants who were declined have no observed outcome (default or repayment). Training PD models only on approved accounts introduces selection bias—the approved population is a non-random subset, typically lower risk than the full applicant pool. According to Basel Committee research, this can lead to overly optimistic PD estimates and suboptimal cutoffs when the model is applied to new applicants.

Common reject-inference methods include: (1) Parceling—assigning rejected applicants to good/bad based on their score rank relative to approved accounts; (2) Reweighting—weighting approved accounts to approximate the full applicant distribution; (3) Augmentation—using external data (e.g., bureau performance on other products) to infer outcomes for rejects. Each method has assumptions and limitations; the choice depends on data availability and regulatory acceptance. 

-- Expected Profit per Applicant (simplified)
-- E[Profit] = (1 - PD) * Revenue - PD * LGD * EAD
WITH applicant_scores AS (
  SELECT applicant_id, pd_score, lgd_estimate, ead_estimate, revenue_estimate
  FROM applications a
  JOIN pd_model p ON a.features = p.features
  JOIN lgd_model l ON a.features = l.features
)
SELECT applicant_id,
  (1 - pd_score) * revenue_estimate - pd_score * lgd_estimate * ead_estimate AS expected_profit
FROM applicant_scores;

Metrics, KPIs & North Star

The PD × LGD × EAD Decomposition

Expected loss for a loan is: EL = PD × LGD × EAD. Revenue is typically interest and fees over the life of the loan. Expected profit = Revenue − EL (simplified; in practice, discounting and prepayment matter). According to Basel guidance, PD is the probability of default over a defined horizon (e.g., 12 months); LGD is the fraction of EAD lost at default (1 − recovery rate); EAD is exposure at default (outstanding balance plus undrawn commitment). Scorecards and ML models can output PD directly; LGD and EAD often come from separate models or rules.

Primary Metrics

  • Expected profit per decision
  • Portfolio NIM (net interest margin)
  • Loss ratio (credit cost / revenue)

Secondary Metrics

  • PD/LGD calibration (actual vs predicted)
  • Approval rate by segment
  • Default rate by segment

Guardrails

  • Fairness (adverse action, disparity)
  • Capital adequacy
  • Model stability (vintage)

Profit-Optimal vs Accuracy-Optimal Cutoffs

Maximizing classification accuracy (e.g., AUC) does not maximize profit. The profit-optimal cutoff is where marginal expected profit of one more approval equals zero. According to McKinsey's credit risk research, this cutoff can be significantly lower than the accuracy-optimal cutoff when revenue per good customer is high and loss per bad customer is moderate—implying more approvals and higher volume. Conversely, when loss given default is very high, the profit-optimal cutoff may be more conservative. Backtesting profit at different score cutoffs is essential.

Analytical / Experimental Approach

Backtesting Profit at Score Cutoffs

The core analytical exercise is to backtest expected profit at different score cutoffs using historical application and performance data. For each candidate cutoff, compute: (1) approval rate (fraction of applicants above cutoff); (2) default rate among approved (from performance data); (3) expected profit per approved account = (1 − PD) × Revenue − PD × LGD × EAD, averaged over approved cohort. According to Gartner's credit risk research, the cutoff that maximizes total portfolio profit (approval rate × E[profit] per approved) is the profit-optimal policy. This can be done segment-by-segment (e.g., by product, channel) to allow segment-specific cutoffs.

A/B Testing Policy Changes

When introducing a new cutoff or new model, A/B testing allows measurement of approval rate, default rate, and profit in a controlled way. Treatment group receives new policy; control receives incumbent. Outcomes (default, revenue) are observed over 12–24 months. According to HBR's A/B testing guidance, pre-register primary metric (e.g., risk-adjusted profit per applicant) and guardrails (fairness, default rate by segment). Use sequential or fixed-horizon design; report confidence intervals. Credit experiments are long-horizon—interim metrics (e.g., early delinquency) can proxy for default.

Vintage Analysis for Model Validation

Vintage analysis—default rate by origination cohort and months-on-book—surfaces model drift and policy impact. If recent vintages default at higher rates than predicted by PD model, the model may be stale or the population may have shifted. Basel Committee guidance emphasizes backtesting and benchmarking; vintage curves are a key tool. Run vintage analysis quarterly; trigger model review when actual default rates exceed predicted by a material margin.

AI & Automation Layer

Automated PD/LGD Model Retraining

PD and LGD models require periodic retraining as new performance data arrives and population shifts. According to Gartner's credit risk research, leading lenders retrain PD models every 6–12 months, incorporating reject inference and vintage-weighted samples. LGD models are retrained less frequently (12–24 months) due to fewer default events. Automation pipelines: (1) extract performance and application data; (2) apply reject-inference; (3) train PD/LGD models; (4) backtest profit curves; (5) document and version for governance.

Profit-Curve and Fairness Dashboards

Dashboards that surface expected profit by score band, approval rate by segment, and default rate by vintage enable continuous monitoring. Fairness dashboards—approval rate and default rate by protected group (e.g., geography, age band)—support ECOA compliance and adverse action documentation. ECOA requires that applicants receive reasons for adverse action; model documentation and score reason codes must support explainability.

Observations & Key Findings

Finding 1: Profit-Optimal Cutoff Often Differs from Accuracy-Optimal

Across multiple lenders and products, the cutoff that maximizes AUC or classification accuracy is often more conservative than the cutoff that maximizes expected profit. According to McKinsey's credit risk research, the gap can be 50–100 score points—approving 10–20% more applicants at the profit-optimal cutoff. The mechanism: marginal applicants (just above profit-optimal cutoff) have positive expected profit; excluding them to improve accuracy leaves money on the table.

Finding 2: Vintage Analysis Surfaces Policy and Economic Effects

Vintage curves reveal whether recent cohorts perform as predicted. A sudden worsening in recent vintages may indicate model drift, loosening policy, or economic stress. According to Gartner's credit risk research, lenders that run vintage analysis quarterly and trigger model review when actual default exceeds predicted by >20% see fewer capital surprises and better IFRS 9 staging.

Finding 3: Reject Inference Improves Representativeness But Adds Assumption Risk

Reject inference methods (parceling, reweighting, augmentation) improve model representativeness to the full applicant pool and can shift profit-optimal cutoffs. However, each method relies on assumptions (e.g., parceling assumes score rank among rejects mirrors outcome). When assumptions are wrong, reject inference can bias PD estimates. Document assumptions; compare portfolio performance with and without reject inference in backtests.

Finding 4: Fairness Constraints Can Be Incorporated as Constraints or Guardrails

Profit optimization can be constrained by fairness: e.g., no disparate impact (approval rate ratio between protected groups above a threshold). According to ECOA and fair lending guidance, lenders must monitor and address disproportionate impact. Options: (1) segment-specific cutoffs that satisfy fairness; (2) post-hoc adjustment (e.g., lower cutoff for underserved segment); (3) guardrail metrics that trigger review when disparity exceeds threshold. Analytics teams should work with compliance to define and implement.

Trade-offs, Risks & Failure Modes

Trade-off 1: Profit vs. Volume vs. Fairness

Aggressive cutoffs (approve more) increase volume and revenue but also default risk. Conservative cutoffs reduce loss but shrink volume. Fairness constraints (e.g., minimum approval rate for protected groups) may require approving some applicants with negative expected profit. The optimal balance depends on strategic objectives, capital, and regulatory constraints.

Trade-off 2: Reject Inference Benefit vs. Assumption Risk

Reject inference improves model calibration to the full applicant pool but relies on unverifiable assumptions. When assumptions fail (e.g., rejected applicants would have performed worse than parceling implies), PD estimates can be biased. Some lenders prefer to avoid reject inference and accept selection bias, documenting the limitation; others use conservative reject-inference methods (e.g., augmentation with external data only).

Failure Mode: Optimizing Only for Default Rate

Minimizing default rate leads to very conservative cutoffs—approving only the safest applicants. Volume and profit shrink. The right objective is risk-adjusted profit, not default rate alone.

Failure Mode: Optimizing Only for Volume

Maximizing approval rate or volume without regard to default can blow loss when marginal applicants default at high rates. Profit and capital suffer. Always tie volume to expected profit and loss.

Risk: Model Drift in Downturns

PD models trained in benign periods may underestimate default in recessions. Vintage analysis and stress testing (apply recession scenarios to PD/LGD) help. Regulators expect through-the-cycle adjustments or point-in-time models with conservative margins in stress.

Quantified Impact

Decision Framework Applied

  1. Adopt profit-based decision framework: optimize for expected profit, not accuracy alone.
  2. Use PD × LGD × EAD decomposition; feed scorecards and models into these components.
  3. Backtest profit at multiple cutoffs; set segment-specific cutoffs where justified.
  4. Run vintage analysis quarterly; trigger model review when actual default exceeds predicted.
  5. Apply reject inference with documented assumptions; compare with and without in backtests.

Limitations & What We'd Improve

Limitations

  • Reject inference is unobservable: True outcomes for rejected applicants are never observed; all methods rely on assumptions that cannot be fully validated.
  • PD/LGD in low-default portfolios: Niche or prime segments have few defaults; PD/LGD estimates have high variance. Use conservative margins or pooled data where appropriate.
  • Revenue and prepayment: Simplified profit = Revenue − EL ignores prepayment, churn, and cross-sell. Full lifetime value models improve accuracy but add complexity.
  • Economic cycles: Models trained in expansion may fail in recession; through-the-cycle vs point-in-time design involves trade-offs.

What We'd Improve

  • Alternative reject inference: Test augmentation with bureau performance on other products; explore semi-supervised and causal approaches.
  • Stress testing: Apply recession scenarios to PD/LGD; set capital and provisioning buffers.
  • Segment-level profit curves: Backtest profit by product, channel, and segment; allow segment-specific cutoffs where data support.
  • Explainable adverse action: Integrate SHAP or reason codes so every decline has a clear, compliant explanation.

Future Scope (12–24 Months)

Causal Credit Policies

Moving from correlation-based scorecards to causal models (e.g., what is the effect of approval on default, holding applicant quality fixed?) could improve policy design. Instrumental variable and regression discontinuity designs are emerging in credit research.

Explainable Adverse Action

ECOA requires adverse action reasons. SHAP, LIME, and reason-code systems will become standard so that every decline has a clear, auditable explanation. This supports both compliance and customer trust.

Integration with IFRS 9 and Basel Reporting

PD/LGD/EAD outputs will feed directly into IFRS 9 ECL staging and Basel capital calculations. Single source of truth for both profit and regulatory reporting reduces inconsistency and audit burden.

ML and Alternative Data

Gradient boosting and neural networks are increasingly used for PD; alternative data (e.g., cash flow, rent) may improve prediction for thin-file applicants. Governance and fairness remain critical as models become more complex.
