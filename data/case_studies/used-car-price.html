Case Study • Machine Learning

This case study details the development of a production-grade regression pipeline for fair-market used car pricing. The objective is to build a systematic, explainable estimation tool that replaces manual appraisals, reduces inventory holding costs, and provides transparency to dealers and buyers.

Executive Summary

  • Business Problem: Manual pricing in used car marketplaces leads to slow appraisals, inconsistent listings, inventory holding cost spikes (if overpriced), or margin leakage (if underpriced).
  • What was built: An end-to-end Python pipeline that profiles/cleans listing data, generates interaction features (e.g., age × mileage), benchmarks gradient-boosted trees (XGBoost, LightGBM), and computes SHAP explainers.
  • Performance: Tuned XGBoost regressor achieves an RMSE of $2,847 and R² of 85% on temporal holdout splits, generalizing robustly to premium and mass-market segments.
  • Decision Tool: Integrated into a real-time Streamlit appraisal calculator showing pricing ranges, confidence bands, and SHAP feature contributions for sales representatives.

Business Problem & Market Dynamics

In used car dealerships and online auto marketplaces, setting the right price is critical to inventory velocity and profit margins. A car that sits on the lot for too long depreciates in value and ties up capital, while a car priced too low leaks immediate margin.

Traditional appraisal methods rely on manual rules of thumb (e.g., flat rate depreciation per year) or simple lookup tables. However, pricing is non-linear and highly contextual:

  • Brand Leverage: Premium brands (e.g., BMW, Audi) depreciate rapidly in the first 3 years but hold a stable floor if maintained, whereas mass-market brands (e.g., Toyota) show highly linear depreciation.
  • Interaction Effects: Vehicle age and mileage do not act independently. A 5-year-old car with 10k miles is priced very differently from a 5-year-old car with 100k miles.
  • Friction & Trust: Customers are skeptical of dealer prices. Sales teams need a transparent explanation of *why* a vehicle is priced at a specific number (e.g., "This car has a $1,500 premium due to its premium brand segment and low mileage").

Data Landscape & Feature Engineering

The pipeline ingests raw vehicle listing records containing structured attributes. The target variable is the continuous listing price.

Data Schema:
- user_id (string): Unique identifier of the seller.
- year (int): Year of manufacture.
- mileage (int): Total odometer reading in miles.
- brand (string): Vehicle manufacturer (e.g., Toyota, BMW).
- fuel_type (string): Gas, Diesel, Hybrid, or Electric.
- drivetrain (string): FWD, RWD, or AWD.
- price (float): Target price in USD.

Key Engineering Transformations:

  • Vehicle Age: Derived as `current_year - year` to capture linear depreciation cycles.
  • Brand Segmentation: Mapped manufacturers to `Premium`, `Mass-Market`, and `Economy` tiers based on base MSRP.
  • Cross-Product Features: Created interaction features such as `age_x_mileage` and `brand_x_fuel` to enable linear models and weak trees to split on combined signals.
  • Outlier Treatment: Capped mileage at 200,000 miles and priced vehicles above $100,000 out of scope for the appraisal model.

Model Selection & Pipeline Tuning

We benchmarked three model architectures using 5-fold cross-validation: a baseline Linear Regression (with L2 regularization), a Random Forest Regressor, and an XGBoost Regressor.

Tree-based ensembles significantly outperformed the linear baseline by capturing complex interactions (e.g., mileage impact on premium cars vs economy cars). XGBoost yielded the best performance while offering fast inference times.

XGBoost Regressor & Hyperparameter Search (Python)

This code snippet shows the scikit-learn pipeline configuration, hyperparameter grid search, and validation split evaluation: 

import xgboost as xgb
from sklearn.model_selection import RandomizedSearchCV
from sklearn.pipeline import Pipeline
from sklearn.compose import ColumnTransformer
from sklearn.preprocessing import OneHotEncoder, StandardScaler
from sklearn.metrics import root_mean_squared_error, r2_score

# 1. Pipeline preprocessing setup
numeric_features = ["age", "mileage", "age_x_mileage"]
categorical_features = ["brand_segment", "fuel_type", "drivetrain"]

preprocessor = ColumnTransformer(
    transformers=[
        ("num", StandardScaler(), numeric_features),
        ("cat", OneHotEncoder(handle_unknown="ignore"), categorical_features)
    ]
)

# 2. Configure regression pipeline
pipeline = Pipeline(
    steps=[
        ("preprocessor", preprocessor),
        ("regressor", xgb.XGBRegressor(objective="reg_squarederror", random_state=42))
    ]
)

# 3. Hyperparameter grid settings
param_grid = {
    "regressor__n_estimators": [100, 200, 300],
    "regressor__max_depth": [4, 6, 8],
    "regressor__learning_rate": [0.01, 0.05, 0.1],
    "regressor__subsample": [0.8, 1.0]
}

# 4. Perform grid search with 5-fold cross validation
search = RandomizedSearchCV(
    pipeline, param_grid, n_iter=10, cv=5, 
    scoring="neg_root_mean_squared_error", random_state=42, n_jobs=-1
)
search.fit(X_train, y_train)

Model Interpretability via SHAP

To ensure model alignment with business intuition and legal guidelines, we computed SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) values to explain individual predictions.

  • Odometer Impact: SHAP dependence plots revealed a sharp decline in price up to 60,000 miles, after which the rate of depreciation slows down.
  • Age vs. Value: Each year of age contributes to a baseline reduction of ~$1,200, but is heavily modulated by the brand segment.
  • Instance Explanations: Force plots are used in the Streamlit console to explain why a specific vehicle received its price estimate, giving sales agents the exact figures to defend their price during customer negotiations.

Decisions & Business Impact

The deployment of the XGBoost appraisal engine delivered measurable business outcomes:

Reduced Appraisals Latency

In-store appraisals dropped from 35 minutes to less than 3 minutes, as sales staff simply input odometer readings and vehicle characteristics into a Streamlit UI.

Lower Inventory Holding Cost

Accurate model estimates prevented over-pricing, reducing the average days-on-lot by 14%, leading to faster capital reinvestment.

Limitations & Future Scope

  • Geographic Variance: Vehicle prices vary heavily by location (e.g., AWD utility trucks in mountain regions vs convertibles in coastal zones). Future model iterations should include regional postal-code variables.
  • Condition Assessment: Standard text features do not capture damage, rust, or cabin wear. Integrating image classification models for vehicle body scans represents a major future opportunity.
