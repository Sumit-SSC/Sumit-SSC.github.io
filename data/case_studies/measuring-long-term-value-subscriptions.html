Subscription businesses often over-index on short-term retention (e.g., Day 1, Day 7) and under-invest in measuring long-term value. This case study examines LTV realism, cohort decay, and delayed impact—using Reforge and McKinsey subscription economics.

Executive Summary

  • LTV realism: LTV is uncertain and model-dependent; use ranges and scenarios (best/base/worst); validate with cohort curves and payback periods.
  • Cohort decay: Retention and revenue decay by cohort age; measure decay curves by acquisition channel and product tier to inform CAC payback and LTV.
  • Delayed impact: Product and pricing changes affect long-term retention with lag; use long-term holdouts and vintage analysis to capture delayed effects.

Business Context & Problem Framing

Reforge's LTV essays and McKinsey's subscription work emphasize that optimizing for early retention alone can miss long-term value—and that LTV estimates are only as good as the cohort data and assumptions (discount rate, horizon, churn model). Measuring and forecasting LTV with realism (ranges, validation) is essential for unit economics and growth decisions.

Data Landscape & Event Model

Subscriptions: user_id, start_date, end_date, plan, mrr. Events: payment, usage, cancel. Cohorts: defined by sign-up period (e.g., month); for each cohort, compute retention and cumulative revenue by period. LTV = expected cumulative revenue over horizon (e.g., 24 months) with churn model and optional discounting.

Metrics, KPIs & North Star

Primary: LTV (with range) and payback period. Secondary: retention curve by cohort, ARPU, churn rate by tenure. Guardrails: early retention (D1, D7, D30) as leading indicators. North star: LTV/CAC or risk-adjusted LTV by channel and segment.

Analytical / Experimental Approach

Build cohort tables and retention/revenue curves; fit LTV model (e.g., survival + revenue); validate with holdout cohorts. A/B test acquisition or product with long-term holdouts (e.g., 12-month LTV). Use vintage analysis to detect delayed impact of changes.

AI & Automation Layer

Automated cohort and LTV reporting; scenario-based LTV (best/base/worst); alerts when cohort curves shift; optional ML for LTV prediction and payback forecasting.

Observations & Key Findings

Short-term retention is a weak proxy for LTV when decay curves vary by segment; LTV ranges and validation reduce overconfidence; long-term holdouts are necessary to measure true impact of product and acquisition changes.

Trade-offs, Risks & Failure Modes

Trade-off: speed (short-term metrics) vs. accuracy (LTV). Risk: LTV model misspecification. Failure mode: optimizing for early retention while LTV and payback worsen.

Business Impact & Decision Outcomes

Decision: measure LTV and payback with cohort curves and ranges; use long-term holdouts for key experiments; report LTV/CAC by channel. Outcome: better growth and pricing decisions aligned to long-term value.

Limitations & What We'd Improve

LTV horizon and churn assumptions are uncertain; new products have limited cohort history. Improvements: probabilistic LTV; segment-level models; integration with CLV and marketing attribution.

Future Scope (12–24 Months)

Causal LTV (effect of treatment on LTV); real-time LTV scoring; subscription health scores that predict LTV.

Key Takeaway

Move beyond short-term retention by measuring LTV with realism (cohort curves, ranges, validation), cohort decay, and delayed impact. Use long-term holdouts and LTV/CAC by channel so subscription strategy is aligned to long-term value.
